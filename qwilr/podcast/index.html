When Big Problems meet Old Wisdom

How different wisdom traditions address social problems

Why might someone prefer living on the street over staying in a shelter? Is the way we punish hate crimes leading to more hate? Could  “therapeutic community” improve our mental health? Are nonprofits making things worse? What are the questions we don’t ask that are preventing us from solving society’s biggest problems?

Join Edmonton’s Historian Co-Laureates: Cheryl Whiskeyjack & Omar Yaqub, as they explore how to respond to some of today's biggest problems: hate crimes, isolation, belonging, injustice & health with youth & the wisdom traditions they represent.

What we want to do…

  1. 1. We want to discuss hot issues in ways that make people think, reflect on their preconceived notions about what different wisdom traditions have to offer & their relevance to today’s social dilemmas.
  2. 2. We want to create formal mentorship opportunities for youth interested in media.
  3. 3. We want to elevate and enrich our civics discourse.

Audience

  1. 1. People who care about public policy - they listen to Templeton Funded podcasts
  2. 2. People from diverse faith communities - they listen to On Being with Kristina Tippit.
  3. 3. People curious about different worldviews and shifting perspectives - they listen to Twisted Histories & 99% Invisible.

Format

  1. 1. Live community driven sessions.
  2. 2. Narrative podcast.

Why

As Historian Co-Laureates for the City of Edmonton and people who strive to serve the community through non-profits we wrestle with intense questions. How do we serve people with highly intense needs while questioning the underlying systems that lead to inequality? How might our traditions help us tackle the problems we see in a better way? How do we go about integrating and explaining different worldviews within colonial systems.

Questions…

Why might someone prefer living on the street over staying in a shelter?

What makes a place home? Is home a roof or a community? What does our current approach to rapid housing miss? Why might people prefer living on the street to a shelter? What are ways of living that are common abroad but missing on our local landscape? What can we learn & apply from all of these  questions as we try to build new (affordable) housing?

Could “therapeutic communities” be a prescription for mental health?

Does everyone need a therapist? We often approach mental health in diverse communities from a deficit/ taboo perspective. What if we tried to learn and build on what’s working? What are the natural assets and approaches to supporting mental health that we can learn from as we look for scalable approaches to addressing rampant anxiety and isolation.

Is the way we punish hate crimes leading to more hate?

Our response to hate crimes is often to push the racialized communities who are over policed towards the justice system. Will that really make people safer or help them heal? Edmonton has seen a rash of hate crimes – there have been more than a dozen attacks on black muslim women in hijab this past year.  Often the perpetrators aren’t who we think; many of the attackers come with their own racialized trauma and difficulties. What might it look like to address a hate crime and healing from the justice traditions of the communities involved?

The people most often impacted by safety concerns are often from underserved & diverse communities. How do they feel about policing and what would make them feel safe? What types of investment are they looking for in their neighbourhood?

Are non-profits making things worse?

Have non-profits actually made things better? Has the dogma of nonprofits changed the way we think about supporting one another? How long have social service agencies been around & what existed before them? What can we apply today to make society better? How can blockchain take us back to Indigenous ways of governance, how can the Muslim institution of zakat disrupt poverty?

Can i be both colonized and a colonizer?

What does it mean to be “Treaty people” for refugees and what are the lessons for all of us in examining our own place in Canada? How does this speak to the larger question of belonging and what Canadian identity is today?


The Team…

Our ensemble includes podcasters, journalists, writers, experience designers, social service workers, restorative justice experts, thought leaders & sacred knowledge keepers.

Cheryl Whiskeyjack – Host & Narrator

Cheryl is the Executive Director of the Bent Arrow Traditional Healing Society and has been with Bent Arrow just about since its inception over a quarter-century ago. Now she oversees a suite of 21 different programs and services. The organization's programming connects indigenous clients to the city around them, and helps them build skills and resilience that they can use in other parts of their lives.

Cheryl proudly serves as a board member of the Canadian Accreditation Council of Human Services, the Align Association of Community Services and was newly appointed to the Board of PolicyWise for Children & Families. She has contributed to the work of EndPovertyEdmonton since its inception and currently acts as co-chair of the Stewardship Round Table. She also sits at the table for the National Advisory Council on Poverty. She acts as a mentor for the Edmonton Chamber of Voluntary Organizations Executive Director Mentorship program and represents Bent Arrow through CS, a collaborative partnership of five non-profits using innovative engagement in vulnerable neighbourhoods. Cheryl is proud of the strong partnerships she has maintained across sectors.

A graduate of the Child & Youth Care Program at Grant MacEwan University in 1995 and was recognized with Distinguished Alumni Award in 2018. She was also recognized with an Honorary Doctorate of Laws by the University of Alberta in 2021. Cheryl was also recognized as a Paul Harris Fellow by Rotary Foundation in 2021.

Omar Yaqub – Host & Narrator

Omar Yaqub DSL MBA BSc (@somaryaqub Twitter , LinkedIn ), serves the team at IslamicFamily, a social change organization that is disrupting systemic inequity through research & beauty. He is the Historian Co-Laureate for the City of Edmonton. He has two decades of experience in the for-benefit sector. During his time with IslamicFamily the organization has become a multi-award winning Imagine Canada & Great Places to Work accredited charity. Omar has been awarded a Doctor of Sacred Letters from St Stephen’s College, EMCN’s RISE award for Community Leadership, and the University of Alberta’s Co-op Mentorship Award. He is a settler on Treaty 6.

Hussain Khan – Producer/Engineer

Hussain Khan is a writer and audio storyteller. He was born in Pakistan, currently lives in Oakland, but is proud to call Edmonton his home. His sophomore projects have been awarded grants and mentorship from Telus StoryHive, and he is a HotDocs 2023 Podcast Development Lab Fellow. Hussain is pursuing his Masters of Journalism at UC Berkeley. You can read his reflections and updates on Substack .

Kathryn 君妍 Lennon – Executive Producer & Researcher

Kathryn 君妍 Gwun-Yeen Lennon is a poet, editor and publisher. She writes to make sense of her connections to place, migration, identity, language, and food. She believes in the power of poetry, and the power of sharing our diverse voices, to help us create more equitable, just and beautiful communities. As a poet, she feels most useful when she is lending her voice to community. As poets, she believes we play a crucial role as gatherers of stories, perspectives, voices, experiences, but also – as gatherers of people, and of communities. She is passionate about building more inclusive, beautiful, and connected communities, through storytelling and placemaking, and has been creating at the intersections of community-building, poetry, and community-arts for over a decade.

She is also the co-creator, co-editor, and co-publisher, along with Kyla Pascal, of Hungry Zine. She sees it as a great honour and privilege to be trusted with other people’s words and art, and to hold space for people to converse, and share their voices, in the form of a literary publication.

She was born and raised, and resides in Edmonton/Amiskwacîwâskahikan, with mixed Hong-Kong Cantonese and Irish settler ancestry.
