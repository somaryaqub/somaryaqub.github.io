Pre-Track · Always-On Social Strategy
Community Awareness & Myth Busting
Not a training track — the awareness layer that comes before any training conversation happens. Short, visual, emotionally honest content for people who have never considered fostering and may have active objections. Local faces. Islamic framing. Low ask. Highly shareable. The goal is not sign-ups — it's making foster care feel familiar, possible, and Islamic.
Instagram ReelsFacebookWhatsApp-forwardableTikTok5 content pillarsAlways-on cadence

Strategic intent: Track A asks people to commit time, open their home, and engage with a government system they may distrust. This pre-track does none of that. It meets people while they're scrolling, in a WhatsApp group, or in the mosque foyer — and plants a seed. It normalises the conversation, dismantles the three or four beliefs that stop most Muslim families from ever engaging, and introduces a ladder of small, achievable actions.

Production principle: Every piece of content should feel like it came from Edmonton's Muslim community, not from a charity. Real faces, recognisable spaces, vernacular language. No stock photography. No guilt as the lead. No statistics as the opening line.

The ask — a ladder of entry points

Each piece of content ends with exactly one ask, calibrated to where someone is. Not everyone will become a caregiver — and that's fine. The ladder captures everyone.

Pillar 1 — "Be Like Halimah"
Aspirational · Islamic identity · Campaign anchor

Frame: Halimah As-Sa'diyya fostered the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ. She wasn't related to him. She chose him. This is one of the most honoured acts in Islamic history — and it was foster care. This pillar celebrates that lineage and invites people into it.

Tone: Pride, not guilt. Aspiration, not obligation. "You could be part of a tradition that stretches back 1,400 years."

Content types: Short biography reels of Halimah's story. "Did you know the Prophet ﷺ was fostered?" hook posts. Local Edmonton caregiver profiles anchored to this frame. Eid and Ramadan campaign versions. One of five pillars — used at campaign peaks, not as daily content.

Pillar 2 — Myth Busting
Objection handling · Direct · One myth per post

Frame: Lead with the myth as the hook — the exact words people say — then give the real answer from a Muslim voice: an imam, a scholar, a foster parent. Short. Never preachy. The person giving the answer should look and sound like someone from the community, not a spokesperson.

Myths to cover (one per post):

  • "Fostering is haram / against Islam"
  • "You can't foster if you already have kids"
  • "The government will take over your home"
  • "This is a Western thing — not for us"
  • "We're immigrants — they'll judge how we parent"
  • "Once they come, you can never say goodbye"
  • "I can't afford it"
  • "You have to be married"
  • "They'll try to change how we raise children"

Pillar 3 — Real Families, Local Faces
Testimony · Community recognition · Alberta-specific

Frame: Short, intimate profiles of real Edmonton/Alberta Muslim foster families. Not polished testimonials — honest, slightly imperfect, recognisable. The family at an Eid dinner with their foster child. The dad picking up both kids from school. Their words, in their kitchen.

Diversity requirement: Systematically represent the breadth of Edmonton's Muslim community — South Asian, East African, Arab, convert, refugee-background, single parent, older caregiver, young couple. Every viewer should be able to see themselves.

Format options: 60–90 sec documentary-style Reel. Day-in-the-life TikTok. Written profile + photo for Facebook. WhatsApp-shareable quote card from the family.

Production and distribution guide
Visual identity
  • Real people, real Edmonton locations — no stock photography
  • IslamicFamily palette: Nabawi Green, Pinecone Brown, New Moon Mist
  • Alegreya for display; Alegreya Sans for captions and body
  • Subtitles on all video (WhatsApp silent autoplay)
  • Square and vertical formats produced for every asset
  • Arabic text used sparingly for impact — not decoration
Channel adaptations
  • Reels / TikTok: Hook in first 2 seconds; no logo until end card
  • Facebook: Longer caption, link in post, community sharing encouraged
  • WhatsApp: Self-contained image or short video; must make sense forwarded cold with no context
  • Mosque screens: Static landscape, 15-sec loops, no audio required
Cadence and planning
  • 3–4 posts per week, rotating across all 5 pillars
  • Campaign bursts: Ramadan, Foster Care Month, back-to-school
  • Local family story: minimum 1 per month, diversity tracked
  • Myth-busting series: one myth per week until all 9 are covered, then recycle
  • Content audited quarterly for tone, diversity, and CTA effectiveness
Community co-production
  • Recruit a small cohort of community content ambassadors — current Muslim carers
  • Provide simple filming guidelines so families can contribute their own Reels
  • Partner with local Muslim photographers and videographers
  • Imam and scholar endorsements recorded in advance for myth-busting series
  • Youth volunteers for TikTok-native content creation
Strategic note — "Be Like Halimah" as a pillar, not a brand

Halimah As-Sa'diyya is universally recognised across Sunni, Shia, and Sufi traditions and across every ethnic Muslim background represented in Edmonton. Using her as a campaign pillar is not a religious argument — it's a cultural identity claim: this has always been ours. The pillar should not dominate the content mix (roughly one-in-five posts) but should serve as the emotional apex of the campaign — the moment someone goes from "interested" to "I want to be part of this." It works best at campaign peaks (Ramadan, Eid, launch moments) rather than as a weekly default.

Design note — why Module A5 replaces "faith and culture in the home"

Teaching a Muslim caregiver how to set up a prayer space or provide halal food is condescending — and it misses the actual training need entirely. The real challenge for Muslim caregivers is far more specific: a child arrives whose relationship to Islam has been shaped by trauma, spiritual abuse, or profound disruption. The caregiver shares the faith — but the child may fear it, cling to it in distorted ways, or be spiritually adrift. That requires a completely different kind of preparation than a refresher on Islamic household practice. The "faith and culture in the home" content moves to Track B, where it genuinely serves non-Muslim carers who are encountering these practices for the first time.

Research finding — UK (BuzzFeed investigation + Fostering Network report, 2019)

In England and Wales, 90% of Muslim children being adopted are placed with non-Muslim families. Research documents that identity disconnection in childhood frequently surfaces as identity crisis in young adulthood. Religious accommodation — halal food and prayer space — is among the first requests Muslim children make when entering care, costs almost nothing to provide, and makes an outsized difference to placement stability and long-term outcomes.

Program model — Halifax, Nova Scotia (CBC News, 2018)

After Nova Scotia's Department of Community Services reached out to the imam of Ummah Mosque in Halifax, mosque-based recruitment began producing results that government outreach alone had not achieved. Mosques consistently outperform mainstream channels when the imam is informed, willing, and supported with talking points and follow-up infrastructure.

Research finding — UK Muslim Fostering Project (Fostering Network, 2019)

The Fostering Network's national study found Muslim applicants frequently experience discrimination from the point of first contact with fostering services. At the same time, many workers lack basic knowledge of Islamic practice, leading to care plans that fail to accommodate halal diet, prayer, or Eid. Both failures are preventable. The report's primary recommendation was mandatory faith literacy training for all fostering service staff involved in assessment and placement.
