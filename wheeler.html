Executive Summary

Alberta has an opportunity to dramatically increase the number of qualified care providers especially for Muslim children — a community-led partnership is ready to make it happen.

Muslim children are one of the fastest-growing groups in Alberta's care system, yet culturally matched foster, adoptive, and kinship caregivers remain critically scarce. This is not a pipeline problem — it is a recruitment and support gap that targeted, community-led investment can close. IslamicFamily and AFKA are proposing a three-year pilot to do exactly that.

This proposal outlines a comprehensive, three-year community-led strategy developed jointly by IslamicFamily and the Alberta Foster and Kinship Association (AFKA) to recruit, train, and support more Muslim foster, adoptive, and kinship caregivers across Alberta.

A Note on Child Protection Data

Estimates on the number of Muslims entangled with Child Protection Services is between 5–10%. This figure is extrapolated from 5+ years of direct work within CS’s Family Resource Network, and consultations with Children’s Services staff. The number has not been formally verified through administrative data because Alberta does not currently track the spiritual identity or faith background of children in the care system. Establishing this baseline is itself an objective of this pilot. Two barriers must be addressed: (1) training CS staff to feel confident and culturally competent when asking families about identity, ethnicity, and faith; and (2) reducing barriers to disclosure in marginalized communities, where families may fear that religious or cultural identity could negatively affect case outcomes.

The Problem

Three interconnected barriers in child welfare

Low recruitment of Muslim care providers

There is a pronounced gap between the number of Muslim children in care and the availability of Muslim foster, adoptive, or kinship caregivers to serve them.

Cultural misconceptions and stigma

Many Muslims believe adoption is religiously prohibited or stigmatized, largely due to misconceptions about the child welfare system and Islamic perspectives on care. Concerns mirror those in Indigenous communities, centering on maintaining kinship bonds and parental connection.

Lack of culturally matched placements

Muslim children are often placed in homes that do not reflect their cultural or spiritual identities. Research demonstrates that culturally matched placements improve stability, identity, and long-term outcomes for children in care.

Collaborative Partnership

IslamicFamily and AFKA — stronger together

This proposal is a formal collaborative partnership. IslamicFamily provides 30 years of community trust and culturally grounded program delivery; AFKA provides province-wide foster and kinship care systems expertise, established caregiver networks, and policy alignment with Children's Services. Together, this partnership bridges community and system — an essential combination for durable, scalable impact.

Manage the central caregiver information hub and landing page

AFKA — Alberta Foster & Kinship Association

Provide province-wide foster and kinship care systems expertise and caregiver navigation support

Connect prospective Muslim caregivers to existing networks, training infrastructure, and provincial support systems

Co-develop cultural competence training modules for non-Muslim caregivers of Muslim children

Support implementation of the Mosque/Community Centre "Caregiver Friendly" Accreditation program

Advise on policy alignment with Children's Services and cross-sector scalability to other cultural groups

Facilitate referrals and warm handoffs between community and formal provincial care systems

Co-lead province-wide scaling and Year 3 sustainability and transfer planning

Deliverables

A comprehensive, four-pillar model

These deliverables address the root causes of underrepresentation and instability for Muslim children in Alberta's child welfare system — forming a jointly delivered, three-year model with a sustainable system of support.

A
Co-Developed Caregiver Recruitment Strategy
IslamicFamily lead · AFKA systems support
  • Comprehensive environmental scan drawing on best practices from Ontario, UK, and Australia — tailored to Alberta's context
  • Community recruitment plan developed with community organizations, faith leaders, and existing Muslim child welfare advocates
  • AFKA integrates the plan with provincial caregiver recruitment infrastructure and ensures policy alignment with Children's Services
B
Training Initiatives
Co-developed and co-facilitated by IslamicFamily & AFKA
  • Community training: engage 50 community leaders and train 20 as advocates — focus on imams, counselors, and community influencers
  • Cultural competence for non-Muslim caregivers: modules to support Muslim children's religious, dietary, and cultural needs
  • Children's Services staff training: mandatory cultural competence modules including tools for discussing identity, ethnicity, and faith with families
  • AFKA co-facilitates delivery through its existing province-wide caregiver network and training infrastructure
C
Public Awareness & Stigma Reduction
IslamicFamily lead · AFKA network amplification
  • Local caregiver recruitment and storytelling: highlight real experiences to normalize caregiving within the community
  • Province-wide PR campaign starting in Edmonton (Year 1), scaling across Alberta (Years 2–3)
  • Endorsements from respected community leaders and scholars clarifying Islamic perspectives on fostering and adoption
  • Multimedia content: video series, family profiles, WhatsApp and social media campaigns
  • AFKA amplifies reach through its province-wide foster family networks and communications channels
D
Community & Caregiver Support Infrastructure
Jointly delivered by IslamicFamily & AFKA
  • Landing page: central hub for information, FAQs, and resources about care provision
  • Direct support for Muslim families: navigation, peer mentorship, and culturally specific counselling — pre and post placement
  • Integrated support groups: safe spaces for Muslim caregivers to share experiences and access resources
  • Certification toolkit: resources, guides, and certification to help community institutions support non-Muslim providers of Muslim children
  • AFKA facilitates warm referrals and handoffs between the community pathway and the formal provincial care system

Why This Approach

Evidence-driven, community-grounded, future-focused

01

Evidence-driven

Aziz Foundation UK research confirms recruiting culturally competent care providers requires sustained stigma-reduction campaigns of at least two years. A three-year pilot is the minimum viable commitment for durable systems change.

02

Strong return on investment

Every dollar invested in cultural matching yields an estimated $3.80 in social return by reducing trauma-related costs. Budget mirrors proven models — MCASS (Ontario) and ICNA Relief Canada — with clear milestones and measurable targets.

03

Future-focused and scalable

Designed to support Alberta's demographic shifts. The research, training, and toolkit produced will benefit other cultural communities, enabling system-wide scalability across the province and beyond.
