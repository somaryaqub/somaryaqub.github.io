Community Recommendations · Edmonton Police Service

Building a Stronger EPS

Six practical, precedented recommendations for a more responsive and community-rooted Edmonton Police Service — drawn from frontline experience, peer police services across Canada, and national research. Each is grounded in the belief that a stronger EPS is good for officers and community alike.

1

Work with community partners to hire an Indigenous Elder in Residence, Muslim Chaplain & Sikh Chaplain

Edmonton is one of Canada's most diverse cities, and EPS has a genuine opportunity to reflect that diversity in how it serves and connects with communities. Indigenous peoples — Métis and First Nations — make up 5.5% of the city's population and are a foundational part of Edmonton's civic life. The 2021 Census places Muslims at 8.3% of the metro population — the city's single largest religious minority, with over 83,000 members across 62+ ethnic backgrounds. Sikhs represent 4.1%, supported by four gurdwaras and a strong tradition of community service. These numbers are likely underreported — the retail footprint of halal food at major grocery stores would indicate a Muslim population well over 100,000.

Together these communities represent one in five Edmontonians — and their populations are growing. An Indigenous Elder in Residence, a Muslim Chaplain, and a Sikh Chaplain would give EPS officers a meaningful, trusted place they could turn to for support or questions — deepening relationships, supporting officers navigating culturally complex situations, and strengthening EPS's ability to serve the full city it protects.

Statistics Canada · 2021 Census · Edmonton CMA

Indigenous peoples: 5.5% — including Métis (2.8%) and First Nations (2.5%). Edmonton already has one of the largest urban Indigenous populations in Canada. Statistics Canada projects Edmonton's Indigenous population will reach between 127,000 and 155,000 by 2041.

Muslims: 8.3% of the Edmonton metro population (approx. 83,000 people) — the city's largest religious minority, representing 62+ ethnic backgrounds across 20+ mosques.

Sikhs: 4.1% — Edmonton's second-largest religious minority, with four gurdwaras and a long tradition of langar (community feeding) and public service.

Police services in Toronto, Calgary, and other cities have made this investment and seen the results. These roles work best when they are fully integrated into EPS — present at briefings, available to support officers in the field, and connected to their communities through genuine, ongoing relationships.

Precedent — Toronto Police Service

The Toronto Police Service embedded Elder and Knowledge Keeper voices throughout the organization as co-developers of solutions. Chief Myron Demkiw credited Elders as essential to healing and strengthening relationships with First Nations, Inuit, and Métis communities.

Toronto Police Service — Honouring Indigenous Elders ↗

Precedent — Elder Outreach with Homeless Communities

A joint initiative between Toronto Police (51 Division) and Indigenous community organizations paired officers with Cree Elder Andrew Wesley to strengthen relationships with homeless Indigenous residents. Officers described the program as transformative.

CBC News — Aboriginal elders team up with officers ↗

Benefits for EPS
  • Support EPS's diverse officers with direct support and through ongoing education and peer dialogue
  • Develop culturally grounded wellness protocols to support EPS staff navigating complex calls
  • Expand EPS's reach and trust across communities that are eager for stronger relationships with their police service
  • Prepare EPS to navigate appropriate community protocols — including what to do should an Indigenous, Muslim, or Sikh officer fall in the line of duty

EPS Response — December 2025

EPS referenced its existing complement of interfaith chaplains and informal relationships with an Imam and a multilingual Muslim woman who supports women specifically. These relationships are appreciated, and are distinct from the community-accountable, integrated roles requested.

Following a June 2023 meeting with Chief McFee, senior staff indicated they were close to bringing on a Muslim Chaplain. As of December 2025, this has not materialized. In October 2023, EPS senior leadership requested a high-level business case for these roles. It was provided. Despite several follow-ups, nothing has transpired. An Indigenous Elder in Residence and a Sikh Chaplain were not addressed. These remain open asks.

2

Report on community engagement outcomes and share demographic data on recruits and promotions

EPS's leadership — sergeants, staff sergeants, and inspectors — are doing meaningful community work every day. And EPS has made genuine commitments to building a workforce that reflects Edmonton's communities. Structured reporting would make that work visible, give those commitments measurable shape, and help leadership invest more strategically in what's delivering results.

What this looks like
  • Annual reporting on not just the number of diverse recruits, but also their graduation rates and reporting on how promotions and management reflect community demographics
  • Quarterly reporting on what was heard through community engagements and what was done as a result
National Precedent — Race-Based Data Collection

The Ontario Government in 2019 directed all police services to collect race-based data in use of force incidents. Peel Regional Police and the Ontario Human Rights Commission built on this through a historic Memorandum of Understanding — now a national model for data-driven policing. Over 30,000 hours of human rights-focused training followed directly from that data.

Peel Regional Police Human Rights Project ↗

Benefits for EPS
  • Give EPS leadership a clearer picture of how leadership is connecting with communities across the city
  • Help EPS align its recruitment and promotion strategy with its diversity goals — and celebrate real progress publicly
  • Provide a baseline for setting meaningful targets and measuring impact over time
  • Help EPS tell its own story more powerfully — with evidence of the real difference its officers are making

EPS Response — December 2025

EPS noted that demographic data on recruits is shared in the annual report, and that more formal community engagement initiatives are tracked through the Community Relations team. 3

Build a community impact assessment into senior leadership decisions

EPS should build a community impact assessment into senior leadership decisions — travel, partnerships, and public affiliations. This isn't about adding bureaucracy; it's about giving leadership the foresight to anticipate how decisions will land across Edmonton's diverse communities, and the confidence to communicate them well. The institutions that get this right don't react — they lead.

Established Practice — UK College of Policing & Kent Police

Community Impact Assessments (CIAs) are formal policy in the United Kingdom, embedded in the College of Policing's official professional practice framework. Forces use CIAs to evaluate how decisions, incidents, and operations affect specific communities before and during deployment. Kent Police requires CIA sign-off by an Inspector and monthly Senior Leadership Team review.

College of Policing — Community Impact Assessments ↗   Kent Police CIA Policy ↗

Benefits for EPS
  • Develop clear internal communications protocols for sensitive community issues — giving officers and leadership a confident, consistent voice at every level of the organization
  • Establish a community communications advisory that includes racialized, Indigenous, and faith community voices — giving EPS early insight before sensitive decisions are made public
  • Prevent issues and protect senior leadership from causing inadvertent harm and damaging trust

4

Invest in community-integrated responses to hate crimes and vicarious trauma

Hate crimes don't just affect their immediate victims — they send a shockwave through entire communities. EPS has an opportunity to lead a more comprehensive, community-integrated response that multiplies its capacity and deepens its relationships with the communities most affected.

Community organizations already hold deep trust with Indigenous, Black, Jewish, Muslim, Sikh, and other racialized communities. A co-response model would bring those relationships into partnership with EPS — extending the service's reach and ensuring that affected communities feel supported, heard, and protected. This would also reduce barriers to reporting for marginalized communities, ensuring better and more accurate tracking of hate crimes.

Training community-anchored support workers to identify hate crimes — rather than asking people to self-report to police — directly connects victims to appropriate supports in their community, removing barriers and trauma while strengthening response. It also improves EPS's access to information and its connection to community.

Model — Toronto Community Crisis Service

The Toronto Community Crisis Service, developed in partnership with Black, Indigenous, and other communities, demonstrated that trauma-informed community co-response builds lasting trust and improves outcomes. In its first year, an Indigenous-specific crisis line received 459 calls. Toronto City Council voted to expand the service city-wide after a successful pilot.

CMHA Toronto — Creating a space for safety and support ↗

Benefits for EPS
  • Partner with community organizations to co-develop response protocols for both reporting and responding to hate incidents — drawing on trusted relationships EPS can build with
  • Support community organizations to provide vicarious trauma care after high-profile incidents, extending EPS's capacity for community recovery
  • Establish direct leadership outreach to affected communities after hate crimes — demonstrating that EPS stands with them
  • Opportunity to engage the Canadian Race Relations Foundation (CRRF) to help EPS communicate about hate incidents in ways that reinforce trust and demonstrate genuine care for affected communities
Progress & Response
Originally asked: Spring 2023 · EPS response: December 2025
Not Yet Actioned
EPS Response — December 2025

EPS noted that the Crime and Trauma-Informed Support Services unit (formerly Victim Services) works collaboratively with the Hate Crimes unit and Community Relations internally, and with the City of Edmonton and community partners externally, to support communities through crime and victimization.

As frontline organizations responding directly to hate crimes in Edmonton's communities, we have not experienced an integrated approach, proactive outreach, or meaningful coordination from EPS. The internal collaboration EPS describes has not translated into a community-facing co-response model. The rebranding of Victim Services is noted — the ask is for what comes next.

5

Co-develop a new community-anchored early intervention program for youth

EPS has real expertise, training capacity, and a genuine desire to connect with Edmonton's youth. The opportunity is to pair that with the deep community roots that organizations working alongside schools already have — creating a model that is more than the sum of its parts.

A co-developed, community-anchored early intervention program would build on what works, extend EPS's reach, and create new career pathways within the service.

Benefits for EPS
  • Establish a meaningful, relationship-first EPS presence in Edmonton schools — where officers are known and trusted before they're ever needed
  • Strengthen coordination between EPS, Edmonton Public Schools, and community agencies — so every referral lands in the right hands
  • Pair EPS training with deep social service integration — giving officers the tools and connections to respond to the full picture
  • Invest in high-intensity preventative interventions for youth — reducing future demand on the justice system
  • Offer EPS a more cost-effective deployment of personnel, while offering schools a broader array of services — an EPS-connected community worker can help a student and their family navigate financial barriers, mental health, and other preventative measures, thereby reducing the need for officer interventions
  • Create a new career stream within EPS for community-embedded youth officers — attracting talent and deepening the service's connection to the next generation

Progress & Response
Originally asked: Spring 2023 · EPS response: December 2025
Not Yet Actioned
EPS Response — December 2025

EPS noted the launch of a Youth Advisory Council in 2026 and an ongoing evaluation of its Young People Strategy (2021–2024). Neither directly addresses the ask for a co-developed, community-anchored early intervention program.

The School Resource Officer program has returned to schools with some positive changes — and that is acknowledged. However, the underlying concerns that led to its original dismissal have not been resolved: a failure to adequately support equity-deserving communities, and the absence of a more cost-effective, community-anchored alternative. A Youth Advisory Council is a consultative structure, not an intervention program. The ask remains open.

6

Work with a third party to review use of force and training practices

A third-party review of use of force policies and training is one of the most powerful investments a police service can make in itself. Peel Regional Police did exactly this — and the results speak for themselves.

National Model — Peel Regional Police & OHRC

In 2020, Peel Regional Police partnered with the Ontario Human Rights Commission to undertake a landmark review of use of force policies and training. The results were transformative: a 15.62% overall decrease in use of force incidents, de-escalation used in 97.3% of all use of force situations by 2023, and over 30,000 hours of human rights-focused training completed. The partnership is now a national model for what proactive, community-confident policing looks like.

Peel Regional Police Human Rights Project ↗  OHRC — Work with Peel Regional Police ↗

Benefits for EPS
  • An independent review is a sign of institutional confidence, not vulnerability. It gives EPS the credibility to stand behind its practices, the data to demonstrate its professionalism, and a clear path to the kind of community trust that makes policing more effective. The EPS officers who participated in developing these recommendations are among its strongest advocates.

Progress & Response
Originally asked: Spring 2023 · EPS response: December 2025
Not Yet Actioned
EPS Response — December 2025

EPS outlined its existing oversight framework — internal review of every use-of-force incident, with external oversight through the Edmonton Police Commission, ASIRT, and the Crown Prosecutor's Office. No commitment to an independent, proactive third-party review of use of force policies and training was made.

The situation has not improved — it has worsened. The fatal shooting of Mathios Arkangelo, a 28-year-old member of Edmonton's South Sudanese community, on June 29, 2024 — with his hands raised — underscores exactly why this recommendation was made. Security footage raised serious questions about whether de-escalation was attempted. His family launched a lawsuit alleging the force used was "unnecessary, unreasonable and without lawful justification." ASIRT's investigation continues. The existing oversight mechanisms EPS cited are reactive. This recommendation calls for something different: a proactive, independent review of the policies and training that govern how force is used — before the next incident occurs.

About the Organizations Behind This Document

Lead Developer

Africa Centre

The Africa Centre is an Edmonton-based organization dedicated to advancing the inclusion, belonging, and full civic participation of African and Caribbean communities across Alberta. Through policy advocacy, cultural programming, settlement services, and anti-racism work, the Centre has been a consistent voice for equity in Edmonton's public institutions — including its police service.

Lead Developer

Bent Arrow Traditional Healing Society

Bent Arrow Traditional Healing Society is a Cree-founded, Indigenous-governed organization in Edmonton that has served urban Indigenous peoples since 1991. Rooted in traditional healing practices and the principle of self-determination, Bent Arrow delivers family support, youth programming, cultural reconnection, and community-based healing services.

Lead Developer

Canadian Palestinian Cultural Association

The Canadian Palestinian Cultural Association brings the voice and experience of Edmonton's Palestinian community to this work — a community with a direct stake in how EPS engages questions of trust, accountability, and institutional decision-making.

Lead Developer

IslamicFamily

The Islamic Family & Social Services Association has been Edmonton's leading Muslim social service organization since 1991. IslamicFamily delivers culturally appropriate mental health, family counselling, newcomer settlement, food security, and community development programs to tens of thousands of Albertans annually.

Lead Developer

World Sikh Organization

The World Sikh Organization of Canada is a non-profit organization committed to promoting and protecting the interests of Sikhs in Canada and upholding human rights and social justice for all. Their participation in this document reflects a longstanding commitment to building bridges between Sikh communities and Canadian institutions.

Advisors

National Advisory

Canadian Race Relations Foundation

The CRRF is a federal Crown corporation and Canada's preeminent institution for advancing race relations and anti-racism policy. Established by the Government of Canada, the CRRF conducts research, develops national frameworks, and supports communities and institutions — including police services — in addressing discrimination and building intercultural understanding.

Policing Excellence

Coalition for Canadian Police Excellence

The Coalition for Canadian Police Excellence is a national network of policing professionals and researchers committed to advancing evidence-based, community-centred practice across Canadian police services. Their contribution grounded the recommendations in what is operationally achievable.

Expert Advisor

Chief Roger Chaffin (Ret.)

Roger Chaffin served as Chief of the Calgary Police Service, where he led significant organizational change including equity and inclusion reform, community engagement initiatives, and a substantive overhaul of use of force training. His willingness to advise signals that these asks are not exceptional, but achievable.

Contributing Officers

EPS Officers

A number of serving Edmonton Police Service officers participated in the consultations that shaped these recommendations. Their contributions reflect a genuine investment in a stronger service and a belief that community trust is not a threat to policing — it is the foundation on which effective policing rests.
