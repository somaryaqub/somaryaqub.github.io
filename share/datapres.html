I
Charter One · The Problem

Funding
in the Dark

The sector was built on paper. Digital systems arrived but didn't change the structure — program-centric, retrospective, siloed. Each funder introduced their own reporting framework. Organizations adapted by building systems that could satisfy multiple funders, not systems that could generate shared insight.

"The system optimized for defensibility, not truth. Data was collected as a condition of access, and rarely benefited the person sharing it."

The cost

What this
failure costs

1

Extractive measurement

Funders demand interagative data collection from thouse in trauma, that they never really use.

2

Data as compliance, not learning

The same information collected seven ways for seven funders. Reports arrive well-assembled and technically sound — but ultimately hollow.

3

Siloed collection

Programmatic data tells you nothing about how services interact or how people (don't) progress.

$15B in federal grants flows to Canadian nonprofits each year — how do we show the impact!?

04 / 12
The human cost

Meet
Sarah.

A mother of three fleeing abuse. Three buses, almost an hour waiting. You can see from the look in her eyes she wants to break down in tears.

You'd like to close your laptop, offer her tea, build her trust. But the script says: how much money do you have left in your account? What's the nature of the abuse? Have you contemplated suicide?

After 30 minutes: she doesn't qualify. After she leaves, you spend another half hour writing it all down in a bloated, outdated system.

Frontline burnout
is a design choice.

05 / 12
Charter One · The Ask

One
Ask.

People should be able to move their data across funded agencies.

Right now, every agency intake is an island. A client accessing housing, then mental health, then employment must re-disclose from scratch every time. Data portability means the person's consent travels with them, rather than stuck behing adminstrative barriers.

Governments already guarantee this for financial records, health records, and academic transcripts. Social services should be no different.

II
Charter Two · Guiding Principles

The People-First
Intake Charter

01

Data belongs to people

Treat it like something precious on loan — not something to store indefinitely or use without care.

02

People should be able to move (& delete) their data

Referrals must be easy. That means consistent, adaptable standards that measure people's goals — not program outputs.

03

Ask just enough

Ask the minimum needed in the moment. Let time and the relationship reveal the rest.

04

Questions should be beneficial

Instead of demanding proof of income, ask if someone qualifies for a program. Same information — but it opens new avenues of support.

What this looks like in practice

What if intake felt
like meeting a friend?

Transform is a trauma-informed intake, case notes, scheduling, referral, and reporting tool — built by a social service agency, for frontline workers.

→

Intelligent recommendations

"Based on this profile, Sarah is eligible for Civida housing." Practical support for caseworkers — one click away.

→

Real-time translation: language, platform, medium,

"Can I sign you up for a bus pass?" — sent in English, received in Punjabi, Arabic, Yoruba. Replies come back translated instantly.

→

Five years of history in two minutes

AI summarizes a client's full journey — key patterns, previous interventions, red flags — so a new caseworker is ready to help immediately.

→

Longitudinal by design

Consent collected upfront enables follow-up months and years later — finally answering what long-term impact actually looks like.

What becomes

The bigger picture

See how services interact to produce outcomes — not just what each program did alone.

→

Longitudinal insight

What funders & policymakers can do

Three
Commitments

Funders are uniquely positioned to lead this change — not by asking for more data, but by asking for data that works differently.

1

Enable data mobility

Give individuals the right to move their data between publicly funded organizations.

2

Invest in shared infrastructure

Fund data litereacy, interoperability standards, analytics capacity, and ethical governance. Without this, the shift from activity to achievement remains out of reach.

3

Prioritize learning over compliance

Reward transparency about what isn't working. Treat evaluation as a tool for improvement — not justification.
