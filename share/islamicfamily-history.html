Introduction

Islamic Family & Social Services Association (commonly known as IslamicFamily) is a Canadian non-profit registered charity headquartered in Edmonton, Alberta.

Incorporated in 1992 — though active in the community in some form prior to formal registration — it is one of Canada's largest Muslim-led social service organizations and operates Edmonton's second-largest food bank. The food bank is among the longest-standing in the city, with uninterrupted operations spanning more than three decades including through the COVID-19 pandemic.

IslamicFamily provides culturally and spiritually sensitive services open to all Edmontonians regardless of religion, background, or ethnicity, with particular expertise in serving Muslim communities. The organization serves more than 5,000 clients every month across Alberta, with reach extending from Red Deer to Fort McMurray.

Mission & Values

"To support the flourishing of the whole person in the community through compassion, support, and advocacy."

IslamicFamily's guiding vision is Healthy Muslim Families and Community. The organization holds that service to others is a foundational expression of Islamic faith, and that addressing poverty, family violence, displacement, and social isolation requires attending to the whole person — spiritual, emotional, physical, and material.

Services are open to all residents regardless of religion, ethnicity, or background. The organization situates its work as rooted on Treaty 6 territory and is committed to culturally appropriate service delivery in multiple languages, including English, Arabic, Somali, and Urdu. Edmonton's Muslim community — one of the longest-established in Canada, with fifth-generation residents — numbered nearly 90,000 by the 2021 Census, up from 19,575 in 2001, a growth that substantially shaped the scale and character of IslamicFamily's work.

Community Representation & Inclusion

IslamicFamily is distinguished within the Canadian Muslim nonprofit landscape by the breadth and depth of community representation embedded in its governance, staff, and volunteer base. The organization has consistently reflected the ethnic, cultural, sectarian, generational, and gender diversity of Edmonton's Muslim community — a community itself among the most diverse in Canada.

Significant contributions have come from Sunni and Shia Muslims, and from community members of South Asian, Arab, Somali, East African, and other backgrounds, as well as Muslims who converted to Islam. This cross-sectarian, cross-cultural composition has been a defining and deliberate feature of the organization since its founding, grounded in the Islamic principle that service and solidarity extend across the internal boundaries of the ummah (the global Muslim community).

IslamicFamily has also maintained strong gender representation across its leadership. Women have held every category of senior role in the organization's history — including President, Vice President, Secretary, program directors, clinical leads, and Executive Director. The current board president is a woman, as is the vice president.

Generationally, the organization has deliberately cultivated leadership succession, with youth programming designed in part to develop the next generation of Muslim civic leaders. The board has, at various points in its history, included individuals from the founding generation, mid-career professionals, and young adults in their twenties — a rare continuity of intergenerational governance for a community organization of its size.

Organizational History

Before 1992

Origins & Pre-Incorporation Activity

The roots of IslamicFamily predate its formal incorporation. In the late 1980s and early 1990s, a small group of Muslim community members in Edmonton began responding informally to growing poverty and displacement among Muslim immigrants and refugees. The earliest activities — distributing food and household goods — took place out of private homes, reflecting the Islamic obligations of zakat (obligatory charity) and sadaqah (voluntary giving) as acts of personal faith rather than organized charity. The precise date of these earliest gatherings and the identity of the original conveners remain subjects of ongoing oral history documentation.

1992–2002

Incorporation & First Decade

The organization was formally incorporated in 1992 under the name Islamic Family and Social Services Association under the Societies Act of Alberta. It was among the first Muslim-led registered social service organizations in Canada. Its founding board brought together educators, community organizers, and civic leaders from across Edmonton's Muslim community, including Dr. Lila Fahlman (CM), one of the most distinguished Muslim community leaders in Canadian history.

From its earliest years, IslamicFamily operated what it described as Edmonton's only fully halal food bank — the first of its kind in the city. Services were offered free to all those in need regardless of faith or background. Early programs included a food and clothing bank, computer and internet training, workshops on effective communication and conflict resolution, and cultural sensitivity seminars delivered to healthcare workers at local hospitals.

The organization's tenth anniversary annual report (2001–2002) recorded over 120 food hampers distributed, workshops attracting more than 300 participants, a blanket drive collecting over 1,000 blankets for Afghan refugees in cooperation with the Mennonite Central Committee, and 100 volunteers contributing 3,400 hours of service. The organization also responded publicly to the aftermath of the September 11, 2001 attacks.

2003–2008

Registered Charity Status & Rapid Growth

IslamicFamily achieved registered charitable status effective January 1, 2003 (Charitable Registration #87383 5193 RR0001), enabling it to issue tax receipts and access grant funding previously unavailable to it.

Food hamper distribution expanded rapidly: from 190 hampers in 2002–03, to 273 in 2003–04, to 414 in 2004–05 — a 50% single-year increase. In December 2003, a confidential counselling program offering culturally and faith-sensitive services through a chartered psychologist launched. IslamicFamily and the Edmonton Council of Muslim Communities (ECMC) co-hosted the first annual Eid dinner at the Boyle Street Co-operative Centre, growing from approximately 350 guests to over 800 by its third year.

2008–2015

Professionalization & Scale

By 2011–12, total revenue reached $565,073, with four distinct program streams: the Essential Needs Program, the Fostering Healthy Families Program, the Youth Development and Parent Education Program, and the Inner City Dinner Program. The Essential Needs Program distributed 1,423 food hampers — a 21% year-over-year increase. The Inner City Dinner program expanded to include Hope Mission and served over 3,000 meals in a single year, drawing coverage from CBC, Global TV, Omni, the Edmonton Journal, and the Edmonton Sun.

2015–present

Rebranding, Research & National Reach

The name IslamicFamily was adopted as a working brand. The organization relocated to a purpose-built facility — The Hüb — at 10545 108 Street NW in Edmonton's downtown core. IslamicFamily became a federally approved Sponsorship Agreement Holder (SAH), supporting the sponsorship of more than 400 refugees since 2015. The food bank maintained uninterrupted operations throughout the COVID-19 pandemic.

A preventative youth program, The Green Room, was named one of the 50 Most Innovative Global Muslim Startups in 2016. In 2024, IslamicFamily and Civida announced a joint land purchase in the Central McDougall neighbourhood for affordable housing designed for larger and extended families. In 2025, documented annual impact included 6,200 food hampers, over 1,000 counselling sessions, nearly 160 refugee sponsorship applications processed, 175 housing clinics, 100+ financial literacy classes, and more than 700 youth engaged.

Programs & Services

Essential Needs

Food Bank & Essentials

Operates Edmonton's second-largest food bank with uninterrupted service spanning three decades, including through the COVID-19 pandemic. Distributes halal food hampers funded primarily through Zakat and Sadaqah, plus a clothing and household goods bank.

Family Support

Fostering Healthy Families

Provides direct outreach support to victims of family violence using a client-centred, multi-disciplinary approach: risk management, advocacy, housing and legal referrals, and the Serenity Group therapeutic support program for survivors.

Mental Health

Counselling Services

Individual and family counselling through registered psychologists with emphasis on cultural and spiritual sensitivity. Launched 2003; grown to more than 1,000 sessions annually by 2025, delivered in-person and remotely.

Settlement

Newcomer & Refugee Support

As a federally approved Sponsorship Agreement Holder (SAH), IslamicFamily supports private refugee sponsorship and provides settlement services including housing, employment, language, and social service navigation. Over 400 refugees sponsored since 2015.

Youth

The Green Room

Preventative youth programming for ages 8–24 focused on identity, belonging, mentorship, and civic engagement. Named one of the 50 Most Innovative Global Muslim Startups in 2016. Includes the Mirrors mentorship program and the Civic Youth Fellowship Training.

Community

Inner City Dinners

Community suppers for Edmonton's inner city population, a tradition since 2002. Over 800 meals per event, engaging multi-generational volunteers. Covered annually by CBC, Global TV, and the Edmonton Journal.

Housing Research

Halal Housing Lab

Canada's first research initiative on housing design for extended Muslim families. Findings informed the 2024 joint land purchase with Civida. Recipient of the 2024 APPI Award of Planning Merit and the 2025 Canadian Award for Planning Excellence.

Additional

Other Programs

Prison programming and re-entry services, financial literacy education, housing clinics, and cultural competency training for governments, law enforcement agencies, and social service organizations across Canada.

Zakat Leadership

IslamicFamily has established a nationally recognized body of work on the effective distribution of Zakat in the Canadian context, positioning it as one of the leading Muslim organizations in Canada on questions of Zakat governance, jurisprudence, and practical application.

Background

Zakat — the third pillar of Islam — is an obligatory annual tithe paid by Muslims on surplus wealth above a minimum threshold (nisab), and distributed to eight categories of eligible recipients defined in the Qur'an (9:60). As one of Canada's largest Zakat-collecting and distributing organizations, IslamicFamily has managed Zakat funds for over three decades, distributing millions of dollars to qualifying recipients across Edmonton and Alberta.

The Effective Zakat in Canada Framework

IslamicFamily developed and published Effective Zakat in Canada — a comprehensive policy framework for Canadian Muslim organizations that collect and distribute Zakat locally. Co-developed through consultation with Islamic scholars and practitioners, the document addresses theological foundations, the eight Qur'anic categories of eligibility, the modern Canadian regulatory environment, and practical guidance.

Zakat is for people, not programs.

Zakat cannot be used to build or sustain organizational programs, even those serving eligible recipients, unless recipients actively authorize the organization to spend on their behalf.

Cash distribution requires care.

In the Canadian context, cash Zakat distributions can inadvertently affect recipients' eligibility for government income supports; the framework navigates this tension with reference to both fiqh and CRA guidelines.

Scholarly engagement is essential.

Proper Zakat distribution requires engagement with scholars trained in the classical tradition and familiar with contemporary applications — not independent Qur'anic reading alone.

Zakat embodies social justice, not only charity.

Zakat is a Qur'anically mandated redistribution of wealth — with documented psychological benefits for both giver and receiver, including the purification of wealth and the building of community solidarity.

The framework was developed through dialogue with internationally recognized scholars including Shaykh Joe Bradford (PhD in Islamic Law, CSAA-AAOIFI accredited; former VP at AlRajhi Bank) and Dr. Katherine Bullock, a scholar of Islam and Muslim communities in Canada, through a three-part podcast series.

Research & Innovation

Community-Based Research

At the outset of the COVID-19 pandemic in March 2020, IslamicFamily partnered with the Edmonton Social Planning Council (ESPC) to design and deliver one of the first surveys of pandemic impacts on social service clients in Edmonton. Key findings: 32% of respondents lacked the resources to practise physical distancing despite understanding health guidance — a structural barrier to compliance among low-income immigrant communities.

The Green Room youth program has also conducted formal youth-led community research, including a 2019 Listening Campaign in which 10 trained youth listeners conducted open-structured conversations with 40 Muslim youth aged 16–24 across Edmonton, identifying four core priorities: promoting understanding across communities; accessing healthy and empowering community spaces; opportunities for self-development; and cultivating healthy relationships.

Digital Innovation & AI

IslamicFamily co-founded Flourishing Systems Inc., a social enterprise and subsidiary, to develop technology solutions for the social services sector. Its flagship product, Transform, is a trauma-informed, client-centred case management platform for intake, scheduling, case notes, referrals, communications, and reporting — grounded in the principle that current social service software is built for funders rather than clients, contributing to the retraumatization of vulnerable people and the burnout of frontline workers.

Transform is built on Indigenous OCAP principles (Ownership, Control, Access, and Possession), applies compassionate design to reduce trauma in data collection, and uses advanced AI capabilities to generate real-time insights and reduce administrative reporting burden. Transform has freed up an estimated 30% of staff time at agencies currently using the platform. The total addressable market for social service case management software is estimated at $4.8 billion in North America.

Facilities

IslamicFamily operates out of multiple locations in Edmonton. Its primary facility, known as The Hüb (Suite 2-786, 10545 108 Street NW), is located in Edmonton's downtown core and serves as the organizational hub for programming, community gatherings, counselling, and administrative operations. A separate food bank warehouse supports the scale of the Essential Needs Program. Additional programming spaces are maintained across the city.

Notable Board Members

Dr. Lila Fahlman, CM (1930–2006)

Founding Director

One of the most distinguished Muslim community leaders in Canadian history: the first woman in Canada to earn a PhD in Educational Psychology; the first Muslim woman elected to a Canadian public school board; and the first Muslim woman in North America to serve as a university chaplain (at the University of Alberta). Invested into the Order of Canada in 2001 — the first Muslim woman to receive that honour — for her contributions to Muslim community development and advocacy on behalf of abused women. Her father, Sied Ganam, was among the builders of Edmonton's original Al-Rashid Mosque, the first mosque in Canada. An Edmonton Public Schools K–9 school in the Allard neighbourhood — Dr. Lila Fahlman School — opened in her honour in 2017.

Howaida Hassan

Director & Chair, Housing Committee

One of IslamicFamily's longest-serving board members, having held virtually every officer role — Secretary, Director, Vice President, President, and Past President — across multiple decades beginning in the 1990s. Currently serves as a Director and chairs IslamicFamily's Housing Committee, playing a central role in the Civida affordable housing partnership. Also a senior transportation and urban planning professional with the City of Edmonton, with expertise in transportation modelling, strategy, and policy.

G. Nabi Chaudhary

Director & President (multiple terms)

Served as both Director and President across multiple decades, from the founding-era board to recent years, representing one of the longest continuous associations of any board member in the organization's history.

Abdul Aziz Khan

President (approx. 2009–2013)

Oversaw significant programmatic growth including the expansion of the Fostering Healthy Families program and the Inner City Dinner program.

Appendix: Oral History Questions

The following questions are intended to guide interviews with individuals involved in IslamicFamily during its founding period and early years, to fill gaps in the historical record regarding pre-incorporation activity and the motivations of the founders.

On Origins (pre-1992)

  1. Before IslamicFamily was formally incorporated in 1992, when did the group first start meeting or organizing? Was there a specific event, need, or moment that prompted people to act?
  2. Who called the first meeting, and where did it happen? Whose home, mosque, or facility was used?
  3. Were you or others connected to a mosque, an Islamic school, or another organization at the time — and did that shape how IslamicFamily was initially structured?
  4. How long did the informal period last before the decision was made to incorporate formally, and who drove that decision?

On the Founding Group

  1. Who do you consider the true founders — the people whose vision and energy made it happen? Are there people who were never on the formal board but who deserve significant credit?
  2. What was Dr. Lila Fahlman's role in the early organization? Was she a founder, a patron, an advisor, or something else?
  3. What drew each founder to this work at that particular moment in Edmonton's Muslim community?
  4. From the beginning, the organization drew from Sunni and Shia communities, South Asian, Arab, Somali, and other backgrounds. Was that intentional from the start — and how was it sustained?

On Early Operations

  1. The earliest accounts describe food being distributed out of a private home. Whose home was that, and what did those first distributions look like — who came, how often, how much?
  2. What were the biggest challenges in the first few years — funding, legitimacy, community trust, physical space?
  3. When did it feel like the organization had truly made it — that it was going to survive and grow?

On Context

  1. What was happening in Edmonton's Muslim community in the late 1980s and early 1990s that created the conditions for IslamicFamily to emerge when it did?
  2. How did the September 11, 2001 attacks affect the organization — its operations, how it was perceived, and how the broader Edmonton community related to it?
  3. What do you wish more people knew about what it took to build this organization in those early years?
