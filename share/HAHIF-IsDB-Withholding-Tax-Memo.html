Canadian Withholding Tax on an Islamic Development Bank Investment into the Halal Affordable Housing Investment Fund

Prepared for
Omar Yaqub, Executive Director, IslamicFamily
Re
Non-resident tax treatment of a proposed IsDB limited-partner investment into HAHIF, whose first deployment is Diamond at Dunluce (96 units, Edmonton)
Date
23 June 2026
Status
Preliminary analysis to scope questions for legal and cross-border tax counsel ahead of the 15 August 2026 first close

Not tax or legal advice. This memo maps the terrain and frames the questions to put to counsel (Pallett Valo LLP and a cross-border tax specialist). The two most consequential points below — characterization of IsDB's interest and the participating-debt-interest rules — are fact-specific and must be confirmed in a formal opinion before close. It should not be relied upon for the transaction itself.

Bottom line

  • There is no Canada–Saudi Arabia tax treaty in force, so a Saudi-resident investor gets no treaty-reduced rates; Canada's default 25% non-resident (Part XIII) rate is the starting point.1
  • IsDB's status as a multilateral development bank likely does not create a Canadian tax exemption on its Canadian-source investment income — Canada is not a member, and the relevant mechanism is narrow.4
  • Because HAHIF is a flow-through limited partnership investing in Canadian real estate, the income is taxed in Canada regardless — but generally on a net basis via Canadian filing, which is far better than 25% on gross.56
  • Routing IsDB's investment as an equity / limited-partner interest (rather than financing) neutralizes the biggest risk — recharacterization as 25%-taxable participating debt interest.2

01 No Canada–Saudi tax treaty to optimize against

The instinct in cross-border structuring is to reach for a tax treaty to reduce withholding. That lever is not available here. Saudi Arabia sits on the short list of jurisdictions with which Canada has no income tax treaty in force, alongside places such as the Bahamas and the Cayman Islands.1 For a Saudi-resident payee, the consequence is that Canada's domestic non-resident rate applies without treaty relief: a default 25% Part XIII withholding on interest, dividends, rents, royalties, and similar passive payments to a non-resident.1 Every optimization discussed below therefore works within Canadian domestic law rather than through a treaty.

02 IsDB's multilateral status probably does not create an exemption

IsDB is a AAA-rated multilateral development bank headquartered in Jeddah, owned by 57 member states.3 It is tempting to assume it enjoys the kind of tax immunity that the World Bank or IMF can have. Canada's mechanism for that — the Foreign Missions and International Organizations Act (FMIOA) — only reaches organizations of which Canada is a member, is implemented through a specific Governor-in-Council order, and even then is directed mainly at the income of officials rather than the institution's investment returns.4 Canada is not a member of IsDB and there is no order recognizing it. On its face, IsDB's Canadian-source investment income is therefore ordinary non-resident income.

Confirm with counsel Obtain a definitive read on whether any FMIOA recognition, sovereign-related relief, or member-state structuring could change this. Do not model the close assuming an exemption.

03 The two-tier structure: IsDB → Fund (LP) → real estate

Under the contemplated structure, IsDB holds a limited partnership interest in HAHIF, and HAHIF in turn invests in the real estate. A limited partnership is fiscally transparent for Canadian tax: it is not itself a taxpayer, and each partner includes its share of the partnership's income, with the character of that income preserved as it flows up.5 Interposing the fund does not shield the underlying Canadian real-estate income from Canadian tax — but "flows through" is the favourable outcome, for the reasons in §4.

What matters most is what IsDB holds. As a limited partner, its return is its distributive share of the fund's net rental income and capital gains — equity-style partnership income, not a payment that could be characterized as interest. This is the structural fact that resolves the recharacterization risk discussed in §5.

04 Withholding on gross vs. net-basis taxation by filing

The practical goal is not to make the tax disappear — Canadian real-property income is taxable in Canada either way — but to avoid being taxed 25% on gross receipts when net-basis taxation is available and materially lower.

  • Canadian withholding tends to bite on passive investment income routed through a partnership. Where the partnership is instead carrying on business or earning net rental income, the non-resident partner is generally taxed on its share on a net basis via a Canadian return.5
  • As a backstop for rental flows, a non-resident can elect under section 216 to file a Canadian return on net rental income and recover part or all of any Part XIII tax that was withheld on the gross amount.6
  • The trade-off is administrative: IsDB will have Canadian filing obligations, and the fund files a T5013 partnership return. The partnership can be liable for withholding tax that should have been remitted on a non-resident partner's share, so the remittance mechanics need to be handled correctly.7

Net of operating costs, financing, and capital cost allowance, net-basis taxation is meaningfully better than 25% of gross rent — so the design priority is to land on the "carrying on business / net rental" side and use the section 216 election where gross withholding would otherwise apply.

05 The participating-debt-interest trap — and why the LP structure avoids it

Canada does not withhold on ordinary arm's-length interest — but it carves out "participating debt interest," meaning a return calculated by reference to revenue, profit, cash flow, commodity price, or dividends, which remains subject to withholding.2 That carve-out is precisely what profit-linked Islamic instruments (musharaka, mudaraba, many sukuk) can resemble. With no treaty to soften it, such a characterization would mean the full 25%.

The two-tier equity structure largely removes this risk at the investor level: because IsDB holds a limited-partnership (equity) interest rather than a debt instrument, there is no payment from the fund to recharacterize as interest. A musharaka-style equity interest maps cleanly onto partnership treatment.

Key drafting point This protection holds only if IsDB's interest is a genuine equity / limited-partner interest and not a loan to the fund dressed up as a unit. The Shariah structure (Mufti Baig) and the tax characterization (Pallett Valo) must agree on this point.

06 The fund's "charitable focus" does not reduce the investor's withholding

It is worth stating plainly: the charitable or affordable-housing purpose of the fund does not flow tax attributes to a foreign limited partner. A registered Canadian charity is tax-exempt, but IsDB earning a return is not the charity. The charitable focus may matter for IslamicFamily's own general-partner position and for the fund's overall design, but it does not lower IsDB's Canadian tax on its investment return.

07 Disposition mechanics (section 116)

The real estate is "taxable Canadian property" (TCP). Because the partnership owns TCP and has a non-resident partner, a sale triggers the section 116 clearance-certificate process — and even a single non-resident partner is enough to bring it into play.5 IsDB's eventual sale of its limited-partnership interest is in the same category, since that interest derives its value from Canadian real estate. This is not a barrier; it means a clearance timeline and a purchase-price/proceeds holdback need to sit inside the fund's liquidity and exit planning rather than surprise the parties at sale.

08 Adjacent benefit: financing-cost deductibility for purpose-built rental

Separate from withholding but relevant to the fund's overall tax efficiency given Diamond at Dunluce is 96 units of purpose-built rental: Budget 2024 expanded the carve-out from the interest-deductibility (EIFEL) limits to cover arm's-length financing used to build or acquire purpose-built residential rentals — broadly, buildings with at least four private apartment units.8 This affects the fund's ability to deduct financing costs, which improves the net income that flows through to IsDB.

09 Structural variables to confirm, and questions for counsel

Two structural variables determine which version of the analysis applies. First, the fund's legal form: everything above assumes HAHIF is and remains a flow-through limited partnership. If a corporation appears anywhere in the stack — the fund itself, or a corporate blocker between the fund and IsDB — the math reverses badly: Canadian corporate tax on the real-estate income plus a 25% dividend withholding on distributions up to IsDB, with no treaty to reduce that 25%. Second, the disposition timeline and section 116 holdback must be planned in advance.

  1. Confirm HAHIF is, and will remain through close and operation, a flow-through limited partnership, with no inadvertent corporate layer converting net business income into dividends.
  2. Confirm IsDB's interest is true equity (a limited-partnership interest) rather than financing, and that the Shariah and tax characterizations are aligned.
  3. Model the section 216 net-basis position against gross 25% withholding, including the fund's T5013 and remittance obligations.
  4. Obtain a definitive read on any FMIOA / sovereign-related relief available to IsDB specifically.
  5. Build the section 116 clearance timeline and holdback into the fund's liquidity and exit planning.

10 Recommended next step

Commission a focused cross-border tax opinion (Pallett Valo plus a cross-border specialist) addressing Questions 1–5 above, scoped to the 15 August 2026 first close. The questions are now narrower than a general "how do we reduce withholding" brief, because the two-tier equity structure has already resolved the largest single risk.
