Foundation Setting
This is foundational work that will set the path for IslamicFamily for years to come. It outlines who we are, where're going, and how we'll get there. 

This doc gives you the chance to see the range of thoughts that have emerged and help refine and decide.

Below are the constraints any good vision, mission, or values statement must respect. Keep them in mind as you review each option — and use the flag tool on any card that you think falls short of one.
Guardrail 1
Accessible to all
The language must not exclude anyone seeking services — Muslim or not, newcomer or fifth-generation Canadian. Insider framing or community-only phrasing closes doors before they can be opened.
Guardrail 2
Anchored in tradition
We are grounded in Islamic values — rahma, karama, adl, amanah. The language should reflect that rootedness without requiring it as a condition of access or belonging.
Guardrail 3
Breadth of community
Our community spans an enormous range of need and background: from pre-arrival refugees navigating displacement to fifth-generation Canadians struggling with identity and mental health. The language must hold that full spectrum without collapsing it.
Guardrail 4
Legible to outsiders
Funders, government partners, and referring agencies need to understand who we are without a translator. Avoid insider language, religious terminology without context, or framing that only makes sense from inside the community.
Guardrail 5
Honest about scope
We should not overclaim. The mission shouldn't promise what IslamicFamily alone cannot deliver. Aspirational language is essential — but overreach is a governance and credibility risk with funders and partners.
Guardrail 6
Durable, not dated
The language should hold up in ten years. Avoid references to current events, specific programs, or demographic trends that may shift. A good mission statement outlasts the strategic cycle that produced it.
Guardrail 7
Distinctive, not generic
"Compassionate whole-person care" could describe dozens of organizations. The language should actually sound like IslamicFamily — specific enough that it could not easily belong to anyone else.

Voices from the process
How the board articulated what flourishing and community mean
The mission question itself emerged from earlier strategy work. These voices shaped how the board understands the three key terms — whole person, flourishing, and community.
On "whole person"
"It's not just mental health or housing — it's someone's relationship with God, their family, their place in the community. All of it."
On "flourishing"
"Flourishing isn't surviving. It's someone who came in crisis and now volunteers here, or sits on a board somewhere."
On the arc
"We want to move people from broken arm to strength training — from needing us to not needing us."
On "our community"
"Our community isn't just Muslims. It's anyone who comes to us — and we serve them with the values of our tradition regardless."
On breadth
"We see pre-arrival refugees and fifth-generation Canadians struggling with identity. The mission has to hold both."
On leadership
"Flourishing for us is also about developing Muslim leadership — people who go on to shape Canadian institutions."

What the board said about what the mission needs to do and who it needs to reach
These are the recurring themes from board and leadership conversations about what the mission statement needs to accomplish.
On who we serve
"The mission can't only be for Muslims. Anyone who walks through the door should feel it's for them."
On identity
"We shouldn't hide that we're Islamic. That's who we are — it's in the name."
On the full spectrum
"From crisis support to community leadership — that's the whole arc we're trying to hold."
On clarity
"A funder reading this for the first time should understand what we do in one sentence."
On values
"Rahma — mercy — is the word. That's what makes us different. Not just compassionate care, but mercy."
On distinctiveness
"It should sound like us. Not like every other social service organization in Edmonton."
