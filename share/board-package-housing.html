The Supply Problem: Larger Units Are Chronically Underbuilt

Canada's affordable housing system has prioritized studio and one-bedroom units. The result: an acute shortage of three, and four+ bedroom units that can house extended or multi-generational families.[10]

The 2021 National Household Survey found that household overcrowding disproportionately affects racialized communities and recent immigrants, with Muslim households among those most likely to be affected.[11] In Edmonton, IslamicFamily's intake data confirms this: the majority of families seeking housing support require three or more bedrooms, and the average wait for an appropriately-sized subsidized unit exceeds four years.

The 2022 Edmonton Housing Needs Assessment noted that larger family-sized units represent the single largest unmet affordable housing need in the city — with demand outstripping supply by an estimated 3:1 ratio.[12]

Three Structural Barriers We Have Now Resolved

1. Access to Capital
Canada's affordable housing finance system is almost entirely debt-based and interest-bearing. For a decade, this locked IslamicFamily — and many other faith-based organizations — out of housing investment entirely.[1] The Halal Affordable Housing Investment Fund resolves this by creating a fully equity-based, Shariah-compliant LP/GP structure that both Muslim and non-Muslim investors can access.
2. Misalignment with Federal Incentive Programs
Federal programs like the Apartment Construction Loan Program (ACLP) and historical Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation (CMHC) financing products require interest-bearing debt as a condition of funding.[2] Working through the CMHC Solutions Lab, we have designed a fund structure that layers grant and equity capital to achieve comparable economics without interest obligations.
3. Inadequate Research on Community Need
Prior to 2022, there was limited Canadian research documenting the intersection of Muslim identity, family structure, and housing need. IslamicFamily contributed directly to addressing this gap through the Halal Housing Solutions Lab and partnerships with researchers studying racialized housing insecurity in Edmonton and nationally.[3]

For most of our history, IslamicFamily has addressed housing need through referrals, emergency support, and advocacy. We have served these roles well. But the data, the stories, and our own experience now make clear: a referral-only relationship with housing is inadequate to the scale of need we face.

We can refer families to the waiting list. We cannot build the units they need. Our caseworkers spend an increasing share of their time managing clients in housing crisis — time that would be freed if appropriate housing existed. Direct ownership changes that calculus fundamentally.[13]

Culturally responsive housing — prayer space considerations, gender-separated common areas, halal-compatible building management — requires an operator with direct community knowledge and values alignment. No existing developer in Edmonton builds for this community's lived reality.[14]

Housing as Long-Term Organizational Resilience
Owning and operating housing creates a long-term, diversified asset base for IslamicFamily. This generates sustainable operating revenue, reduces dependence on grant funding cycles, and positions the organization as a permanent community infrastructure provider rather than a project-based charity.[15]
⭐
Zakat Demands Systemic Change, Not Piecemeal Relief
Our own research on effective zakat distribution in Canada found that piecemeal, transactional zakat distribution — giving small amounts to many individuals — fails to address the structural poverty that creates recurring need. This fund channels zakat toward systemic disruption.[16]

Faith Framework

Zakat as a Tool for Structural Change

The Obligation and the Opportunity

Zakat is the third pillar of Islam, an obligation of purification that calls for wealth to move from those who have to those in need. In the Canadian context, the Muslim community collectively distributes an estimated $500 million or more in annual zakat.[17]

For most of IslamicFamily's history, we have distributed zakat as direct financial assistance: emergency rent supplements, utility payments, food access. These interventions matter. But they do not disrupt the structural conditions that bring families back to our door, year after year, with the same unresolved needs.

IslamicFamily's work on effective zakat in Canada, developed through a multi-year collaboration with scholars and practitioners, concludes that the intent of zakat is not to sustain poverty but to eliminate it.[16] Channeling zakat as a community-based rent supplement for the Dunluce project achieves exactly this: a recurring, restricted philanthropic stream that makes rents genuinely affordable for families in need, while generating operating sustainability for the building.

How Zakat Functions in This Fund

The Dunluce project's financial model includes a Zakat Rent Supplement of approximately $100,000 per year.[18] This is not a one-time grant, it is a recurring, community-based contribution that operates as follows:

①
Community Collection
IslamicFamily collects and distributes zakat from the Canadian Muslim community, including from donors who specifically want their zakat used for housing access — an established eligible category under fiqh for those "in need of shelter."[16]
②
Rent Supplement
Eligible families receive a direct rent reduction, paid by zakat funds to the building operator. Rents for eligible families range from 50–80% of market, depending on household income and grant outcomes.
③
Operational Sustainability
The zakat supplement fills the gap between deeply affordable rents and the building's operating costs, creating a sustainable financial model without requiring tenants to pay market rents or the building to depend solely on government subsidies.[18]
📖
IslamicFamily's Position on Effective Zakat

Our 2022 research paper, Effective Zakat in Canada (co-developed with Dr. Katherine Bullock and reviewed by scholars of Islamic finance), concludes that zakat distributors have an obligation to think structurally — not just transactionally — about poverty alleviation. Using zakat for community housing represents one of the highest-impact, most jurisprudentially defensible applications of this obligation in the Canadian context. See full paper at share.islamicfamily.ca/effectivezakat.[16]

Investment Vehicle

The Halal Affordable Housing Investment Fund

Canada's first institutional-quality, Shariah-empowered housing investment fund — developed over three years by IslamicFamily and SVX, with legal counsel from Pallett Valo LLP and Shariah oversight from Mufti Zain Baig. Full details at halalfund.ca.

Understanding Shariah-Compliant Finance

Shariah-finance is a global financial system managing over US$4 trillion in assets worldwide.[9] Its core principles: equity participation, risk sharing, and asset-backed investment are not unfamiliar to impact investors. They mirror structures investors already use: joint ventures, profit-sharing partnerships, and equity co-investments.

What distinguishes this system is that these structures are embedded in an ethical framework that prioritizes fairness, transparency, and real economic activity. Three key principles govern the fund:

I
No Riba (Exploitative Interest)
Returns must come from real economic activity: trade, investment, shared enterprise & not from charging interest on money itself. The fund uses no interest-bearing instruments of any kind.[19]
II
No Gharar (Excessive Uncertainty)
Contracts must have clear, transparent terms. Excessive ambiguity or disproportionate risk-bearing is not permitted. All fund terms are publicly documented and independently reviewed.[19]
III
No Haram Activities
Capital flows only to socially constructive sectors. The fund's investment criteria explicitly exclude industries incompatible with Islamic ethics and prioritize community-benefit housing.[19]

Decision Milestones & Risk

Milestones, Obligations, and Risk at Each Stage

This project unfolds through a series of discrete, board-authorized milestones — each with clearly defined financial obligations, legal commitments, and risk profiles. The stepped structure means the board is never asked to approve the whole project at once. Each gate requires a fresh decision.

🏛️
Risk Track A
IslamicFamily as Organization

IslamicFamily's primary exposure is reputational, not financial. The organization's general operating budget — staff salaries, program delivery, community services — is completely insulated from this transaction at every stage.

No general funds are pledged, co-signed, or at risk. The organization's registered charity status is protected by the structural separation between IslamicFamily (operator and Class 2 LP holder) and the HAHIF fund entity.

The reputational risk — that the project underperforms or stalls, and IslamicFamily is associated with that outcome — is real. It is mitigated by the stepped decision structure, independent oversight (SVX, Mufti Zain Baig, owner rep), and the board's retained authority to approve or withhold approval at each gate.

Key Protections
  • ✓ No general operating funds pledged at any milestone
  • ✓ Legal separation between charity and fund entities
  • ✓ Board retains approval authority at each major gate
  • ✓ Independent owner representative appointed before OTP execution
  • ✓ Legal review of all transaction documents before any signing
📊
Risk Track B
HAHIF Fund & Investors

Fund and investor risks are real financial risks — but they are housed entirely within the HAHIF LP structure, separate from IslamicFamily's organizational finances. Investors in the fund accept these risks as part of an accredited, exempt-market investment.

The most significant investor risk arises after the OTP is executed: if financing is not fully secured before occupancy, the property would need to be sold to repay the owner — and investor capital could be at risk. This risk is mitigated by the multi-year, phased capital raise and the requirement for board approval at each financing gate.

The stepped structure — with three separate board approval points — ensures that no single decision commits the fund or its investors irrevocably to the full capital requirement.

Key Mitigations
  • ✓ $11.1M government anchor capital in place before investor dollars enter
  • ✓ Multi-year phased raise — investors not committed to full amount upfront
  • ✓ SVX professional fund management with securities compliance oversight
  • ✓ Class 0 principal-return option for risk-averse catalytic investors
  • ✓ Board approval required at each major financing decision point

Regulatory & Compliance Risk

Operational Risk (Post-Occupancy)

Fiduciary Framework: What Directors Must Satisfy Themselves Of

Under the Alberta Societies Act and common law duties applicable to nonprofit directors, board members are expected to exercise the care, diligence, and skill of a reasonably prudent person. In the context of this motion, that requires each director to be satisfied on the following points:[22]

⚖️
Duty of Care
Directors must be reasonably informed before voting. This package, the fund overview deck, and the term sheet are the primary information sources. Directors should raise any outstanding questions before voting — not after.
🎯
Duty of Loyalty
Directors must act in IslamicFamily's best interests. Any director with a conflict of interest related to this transaction (investor, developer relationships, etc.) must declare it and recuse from the vote. Conflicts should be declared before discussion begins.
🏛️
Duty to the Mission
As a registered charity, IslamicFamily's activities must serve its charitable purposes. Directors should satisfy themselves that the housing initiative is consistent with IslamicFamily's objects as filed with CRA — community service, social welfare, and support for the Muslim community in Canada.
📌
Conflict of Interest — Directors Should Declare Before Discussion

Any director who is a prospective investor in HAHIF, has a relationship with Vivid Homes, SVX, or any service provider to the fund, or has any other interest in the transaction should declare that interest to the Chair before the motion is discussed. The Chair should ensure declarations are recorded in the minutes. Directors with material conflicts should recuse from the vote. This is standard governance practice and protects both the organization and individual directors.

Fund Structure & Financial Questions

This reflects the two-tranche structure of the purchase. The fund's $5.67M initial contribution supports the construction phase, with the remainder of the capital stack drawn from grants and government programs. However, the Offer to Purchase is structured as a deferred acquisition: IslamicFamily purchases the building from Vivid Homes at a pre-agreed price — approximately $17M — in two tranches.

The first tranche (~$17M, closing March 2028) pays out the initial purchase price. The second tranche (~$15M, around 2032) pays out Vivid's retained owner equity to complete full ownership transfer. The $25M fund target is sized to cover these two tranches alongside operating costs and contingencies, while also being a credible institutional fund size that signals seriousness to investors.

The gap between what the fund raises and what the grants/government programs contribute is the essential blended finance structure: the fund fills the gap that grants cannot, while grants fill the gap the fund cannot afford to offer returns on.

2.5% above principal return is modest by conventional real estate standards — but this is not a conventional real estate fund. It is positioned in the impact investing market, where investors explicitly accept below-market returns in exchange for measurable community impact. SVX's experience is that accredited investors in this market — foundations, religious institutions, individual impact investors — frequently invest at rates well below commercial real estate returns precisely because return of capital plus modest yield is better than a donation (which returns nothing financially).[20]

The Class 0 option (principal return only) is specifically designed for those whose primary motivation is impact — foundations with program-related investment programs, zakat-oriented funders who want their capital to work twice (generate housing impact and return for redeployment), and community members who want to support the project without pure donation.

The honest answer to "will we find investors" is that the August 15 milestone is the real test. SVX's market sounding to date indicates sufficient interest to reach the first-close target. But this is not certain — it is what the next three months of active outreach will determine.

IslamicFamily's financial exposure as an organization is limited to its Class 2 LP position — its equity stake in the fund reflecting its non-cash contributions (land advocacy, community relationships, project stewardship). If the project underperforms, the Class 2 position may generate less residual value — but IslamicFamily is not contributing cash to that position and has no obligation to make up a shortfall.

As operator, IslamicFamily would be responsible for building management under a service agreement. That role carries operational responsibility but the financial terms of the management agreement are structured to be cost-neutral to IslamicFamily's general operations — the building's operating budget covers property management costs.

The scenario where rental income is materially below projection (say, 80% occupancy instead of 96%) would reduce the cash surplus available to service investor distributions — but the building would remain operational and continue to house families. The financial model has been stress-tested at lower occupancy rates and remains viable above approximately 85% occupancy with the Zakat supplement in place.

The Zakat supplement is not guaranteed — it is a projected contribution based on IslamicFamily's established annual Zakat collection and distribution. It is a real and recurring revenue stream (IslamicFamily currently distributes Zakat every year), but it is variable by nature and not contractually locked.

This is an important honest risk. The financial model treats it as a base assumption, not a guarantee. The $100,000 figure represents a conservative estimate of what could be directed specifically to rent supplements from IslamicFamily's Zakat pool — well below total annual Zakat distributed.

The building's financial model has been tested excluding this supplement to ensure it remains operationally viable. Management has conducted a sensitivity analysis showing operating performance at $0, $50K, and $100K Zakat supplement levels.

Operational & Capacity Questions

This is one of the most important questions management needs to answer honestly. Operating 96 residential units is a materially different capability than running social service programs. It requires property management expertise, maintenance systems, tenant relations capacity, financial administration, and regulatory compliance (Alberta residential tenancies, CRA, building code) that IslamicFamily does not currently have at scale.

The plan is to (1) contract a property management; (2) develop tenant selection and management policies; (3) establish financial systems for building operations separate from general charity accounts; and (4) build the community programming layer that differentiates this housing from a conventional landlord.

Management acknowledges this as an open item that requires a dedicated capacity plan before the OTP is fully executed. A staffing and operational readiness plan will be brought to the board as part of the Gate 2 approval package in 2027. The board should treat this as a condition of ongoing management confidence in the project.

Between now and August 2026, the primary management time cost is coordinating with SVX on investor outreach and managing the legal review process — estimated at 15–20% of the ED's time and minimal for other staff. This is a real cost that the board should acknowledge explicitly.

From the fund launch through occupancy (2026–2028), the demand will increase as IslamicFamily needs to participate in governance, provide community context for investors, and develop the operational infrastructure for the housing. Management estimates this at approximately 20–30% of the ED's strategic time — which is significant and requires either protecting that capacity or adding it.

The board should ask management to produce a time-allocation plan before the OTP is executed, showing how existing program obligations will be maintained alongside the housing project. This is an appropriate condition on the final purchase decision at Gate 2.

Shariah Compliance & Community Questions

Shariah compliance review is being conducted by Mufti Zain Baig, an independent Islamic finance scholar. The review covers the fund's LP/GP structure, the Musharakah framework for capital deployment, the return structure for investors, and the absence of any interest-bearing instruments.

Mufti Zain Baig is independent of IslamicFamily and SVX — he is engaged specifically as a Shariah advisor, not as an employee or affiliated party. His review and any written fatwa or certification will be documented and available to investors and to the community.

The board should confirm, before the OTP is fully executed, that the Shariah review has been completed and that a written certification is in hand. Management will report completion of this review as part of the fund updates.

IslamicFamily's mandate is to serve Muslim and broader communities — and the Dunluce project is designed with that breadth in mind. The fund's investment criteria do not restrict housing to Muslim families; they prioritize larger family-sized, affordable units for underserved households. The tenant population will include Muslim and non-Muslim families who qualify under affordability criteria.

The Shariah-compliant financing structure is a feature for Muslim investors and an asset for faith-aligned capital broadly. It does not exclude non-Muslim families from tenancy, and it does not restrict the building to religious use.

The honest communication challenge is framing the fund's motivation accurately: the data shows Muslim and racialized communities are disproportionately affected by housing shortage. Building housing specifically designed for larger families in these communities is responsive to documented need, not preferential treatment. The board should be prepared to articulate this distinction in public communications about the project.

Strategic & Long-Term Questions

IslamicFamily's role as operator is governed by a service agreement with the fund, not a permanent, irrevocable commitment. If IslamicFamily determined it could not continue as operator, the fund could engage an alternative property management organization.
