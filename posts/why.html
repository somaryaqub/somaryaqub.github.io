Why the Social Services Sector Needs to Leave Technology Innovation to the Corporate Establishment‽

We need to find ways to fit people with deep trauma and the social workers they go to for help into established corporate IT systems. We should look to corporate tech behemoths & governments to help navigate emerging issues & identify technology best practices for the social services sector rather than letting the wisdom of frontline workers derail established innovation patterns.

by Omar Yaqub, ICD.D DSL(HON) MBA BSc

01The Status Quo Has Led to Exceptional Results…

The social services sector has had record growth. Demand for services keeps going up. Things are working great! Research done by IBM & CIRA (Canadian Internet Registration Authority) indicates software is a high point. Social workers love the tools/tech they use so much that they spend more than half their time with them doing admin related work; double the amount of time they spend on direct client interaction. Furthermore a whooping 56% of agenicies use CRMs only for donor management and not for engaging clients – indicating there isn't a need and that we’ve hit peek tech utilization.

02Corporate Tech Firms are Known For Frugality & Making Resources Stretch…

If we want to develop something fast and cheap we should look to the corporate IT world. The social services sector cannot be expected to develop tools on tight budgets and with constrained resources. Software development carries a lot of costs; big corporations know how to minimize costs for the sector. Big firms are benevolent, they provide products for basically free to appease our hyper socialist society. A great example of corporate beneficence is the subsidization of low-cost printers – these bring people into the world of self-publishing, the pass through ink catridge fees that follow are inconsequential.

Furthermore, professionals would much rather work with other software pros rather than give back to frontline agencies that may have helped them settle in the country or supported them through difficulty.

“Big firms are benevolent... they subsidize printers and dispense ink cartridges at inconsequential prices.”

03Funders & Corporations Know the Needs of the Social Sector…

The experts the social service sector needs to listen to are clearly those with the skills to build software for the private sector and work in government middle management, not frontline workers. These groups are distinct and should never mingle. Lived experience clouds judgment and takes objectivity away. Having engineers design from a spec sheet and build without the interference of the staff who use the product day to day ensures that the product is free from the untoward influence of end users and staff. Silos are necessary to preserve the integrity of the status quo. Engineers should only talk to reporting/operations managers, who in-turn should stay distant from frontline workers. Information and design direction needs to flow down like a wheelbarrow gracefully gliding down Niagara Falls!

04We Should Ape Government Practices, Not The Start-Up Sector…

People love government IT & the pace government operates. Who hasn’t downloaded a government PDF and marveled at the way it helps heighten focus and demand attention. The social services sector should continue to work with a government-like approach to contract software providers.

The start-up sector with its fancy gamification, and consumer friendly approach is a flash-in-the-pan novelty. We need to let these kids grow up, get serious and make software that feels like pushing a boulder uphill – we need character development not games.

Trying something new is risky, to mitigate the risk we should devote a minimum of 12 months to bi-weekly meetings to discuss the issues before we plan to implement. The sector is exceptional at holding and convening meetings. Meetings allow us to strengthen our abstract reasoning skills in a way prototypes never will. We must keep our lofty perch, and stick to a lengthy production cycle with exceptional gatekeeping. We need to avoid the temptation to commit resources to quick prototypes/testing and stick to meeting cycles that span years and produce wonderful, lucid reports that support the endangered shelving industry.

05The Market, Not Social Services, is Where Innovation Happens…

Nonprofits need to keep their focus on the work they’re supposed to do and not think at a systems level. Funding frontline agencies to develop software will set a precedence. It may lead to non-profits building solutions for each other rather than relying on grants to buy solutions from the private sector. Social services developing their own tools is disruptive and dangerous. It’s not the role of funders / the sector to embrace new tools. We should be apprehensive of internal change. The lived experience and desire to disrupt systemic inequity common in social services deeply clouds judgement. As many of us know, Salesforce, Microsoft and their illustrious ilk have demonstrated a stronger commitment to helping marginalized communities than many of the frontline social service organizations out there.

06The Social Services Sector Doesn’t Have the Experience or Know-How…

We all know there aren’t enough settlement practitioners with tech experience nor tech professionals interested in supporting good causes. We know most foreign-trained professionals are living their best life, and fully employed. Settlement agencies would have a difficult time finding underemployed talent who wants to make a difference and contribute back to society.

07Staff/Clients Need To Learn To Adapt to Corporations…

If we want to set staff and clients up for success we need to help them get used to the soul crushing architecture of corporate software and the way business systems are designed. Adapting the tools to meet the needs of people and making things more empathetic is setting people up for future disappointment. Clients & staff have to learn how to use corporate software systems that are hostile to them for their own good. Long term adaptation to corporate norms is the best recipe to ease turnover and burnout.

08Social Media is a Cesspool that We Should Not Descend to…

We know that our clients and our staff use social media too much. More than 75% of the people we serve get the majority of their information from social media. More than half of social workers regularly communicate with clients over platforms we have no control of: Whatsapp, et al. This needs to stop! Social media is a cesspool and we can’t in good conscience support people using or communicating over these easily accessible channels of filth!

While research has shown that the most effective suicide deterrent is intermittent text messages from a support worker. We also know that social media spreads disinformation/misinformation. We can’t combat this by descending to peoples’ level.

09If We Don’t Talk About Privacy/Security it Will Go Away…

The data privacy of clients is a serious issue that is best dealt with by keeping silent about the issue. If the issue is brought out into the open it will scare people and undermine confidence in the sector. The best way to serve vulnerable clients who trust us with incredibly sensitive information about the worst moments of their life is to treat the issue as if it was a petulant child throwing a tantrum. We need to ignore the issue until it goes away.

10We Can Beat AI’s Encroachment Through Existing Data Practices…

AI is attacking many sectors and automating repetitive tasks everywhere. Social services can resist this scourge by keeping data siloed. Every program should collect its own data, in its own format, on its own timeline, each piece answerable to a single funder who can read the report after it’s matured like fine cheddar.

If we had interoperable data and compared programs side by side, someone might notice a pattern, or worse yet, clients could move their data between agencies without the necessary friction.

Demanding exhaustive disclosure from someone in acute trauma signals rigour. Whether the resulting spreadsheet is ever read, analyzed, or acted upon is the privileged purview of the priestly class: funders. Collecting only what’s needed, and helping clients with data mobility risks turning transactional work relational.
