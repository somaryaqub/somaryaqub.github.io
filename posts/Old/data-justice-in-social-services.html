The Framing Problem

We collect data on the most vulnerable people in our communities — their trauma histories, their immigration status, their mental health diagnoses, their housing instability. Case workers enter it into systems. Funders request it in reports. Researchers analyze it in papers.

But ask a simple question: who does this data actually serve?

In most social service organizations, the honest answer is: funders, first. Regulators, second. The organization itself, third. And the people whose lives generated the data? Somewhere further down the list, if they appear at all.

The Compliance Trap

This isn't malicious. It's structural. The funding relationship creates a reporting obligation. The reporting obligation demands data. The data demand shapes intake forms. And intake forms — with their mandatory fields, their clinical language, their implicit assumptions — shape the first interaction a person in crisis has with your organization.

We've built a system where a refugee family's first conversation with a settlement worker is partly about populating a database that will eventually justify next year's grant renewal.

The intake form is not neutral. It is a policy instrument disguised as a administrative task.

What Data Justice Actually Requires

Data justice, as a framework, asks us to examine the power relationships embedded in how data is collected, stored, analyzed, and used. In the social services context, this means asking:

  • Does the person being served have meaningful access to their own record?
  • Do they understand what's being collected and why?
  • Does the data collected actually improve the service they receive — or just the report the funder gets?
  • Who has the ability to correct errors? To request deletion?

Most case management systems fail all four tests. Not because the people building them are indifferent — but because the incentive structure was never designed around the client's interests.

A Different Starting Point

The People-First Data Charter emerged from a simple provocation: what if we designed our data practices starting from the client's dignity rather than the funder's dashboard?

It's not a technical document. It's a values statement that forces a reckoning with who data collection is actually for. And when you put it in front of a room full of social service workers, something interesting happens — most of them already knew this. They've been living the contradiction for years.

The Charter doesn't solve the structural problem. But naming the problem clearly is the prerequisite for solving it.
