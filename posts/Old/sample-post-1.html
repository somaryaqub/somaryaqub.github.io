Most nonprofits collect data because funders ask for it — not because it helps the people they serve. That asymmetry has real consequences for how organizations operate, what they measure, and ultimately, who benefits from the information gathered.

The Funder-Driven Data Problem

When an organization collects data primarily to satisfy grant reporting requirements, something subtle but important happens: the data becomes about the funder's questions, not the community's needs. Intake forms ask for demographics that map to program categories. Outcome reports track what's easy to count rather than what matters.

The data we collect shapes the story we can tell. And right now, most of that story is written for funders, not for families.

This isn't a critique of individual organizations — it's a systemic problem. The incentive structure of the nonprofit sector pushes organizations to invest in reporting infrastructure rather than client-facing infrastructure.

What People-First Data Looks Like

A people-first data practice starts with a different question: what do the people we serve need to know, and what do we need to know to serve them better?

This reorients several things at once:

  • Intake questions become orientation tools rather than classification exercises
  • Case notes become continuity infrastructure rather than audit trails
  • Outcome data becomes a feedback loop for program improvement rather than a compliance artifact

The Path Forward

Organizations that want to move toward better data practices often face a practical constraint: the tools available to them were built for compliance, not for service delivery. Case management platforms designed around funder reporting categories make it structurally difficult to capture the things that actually matter.

That's the problem we've been working on at IslamicFamily — and it's what led to building Transform, a case management platform designed from the ground up around people-first principles.

The first step, though, isn't a new platform. It's a policy commitment: we will only collect data that we can explain to the person it's about, in terms that make sense to them. That principle alone changes everything downstream.
