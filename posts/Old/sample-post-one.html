There's a quiet power imbalance embedded in how funders collect data from the communities they serve. Grant applications ask us to quantify trauma, describe vulnerability, and enumerate crisis — and then that data disappears into dashboards that the community never sees.

This isn't a neutral act. Data collection without return is extraction. And when the subjects of that data are communities already navigating under-resourcing, systemic exclusion, or displacement, the stakes are higher still.

What we talk about when we talk about data justice

Data justice, as a framework, asks three things: Who collects? Who benefits? Who decides? Applied to the social services sector, these questions reveal uncomfortable patterns.

Most funders — foundations, government departments, intermediaries — collect detailed outcome data from grantees. Program participation rates. Housing stability metrics. Employment pathways. This data feeds annual reports, impact summaries, and strategic plans. It influences where future dollars go.

But the communities that generated that data? They rarely see it. And they almost never get to contest it.

The charter as a corrective

The People-First Data Charter was designed to name this problem and propose a different posture. Not a legal instrument, but a shared commitment: that data collected from our communities should serve our communities first.

It asks signatories to commit to transparency about what data they collect, meaningful consent processes, community access to aggregated findings, and a pause before sharing data with third parties — even well-meaning ones.

The response has been instructive. Organizations that work closest to communities adopt it readily. Funders take longer. Some never do. That gap is itself a data point.
