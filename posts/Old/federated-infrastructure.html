The Duplication Problem

Visit any mid-sized Canadian city with a meaningful Muslim population and you'll find a familiar pattern: a handful of social service organizations, each with their own intake system, their own donor database, their own case management spreadsheets, their own grant applications to the same three funders.

They are often aware of each other. They sometimes refer clients to each other. They occasionally collaborate on specific programs. But structurally, they operate as independent units — competing rather than federating.

The cost of this fragmentation is paid by clients, who re-tell their stories at every referral. By staff, who reinvent wheels that were already built elsewhere. And by the sector's credibility, which suffers every time an organization folds or struggles publicly with a crisis that shared infrastructure might have prevented.

The Credit Union Model

Credit unions solved a structurally similar problem. Individual credit unions are locally governed, member-owned, and community-rooted. But they federate: shared back-office systems, shared ATM networks, shared liquidity facilities, shared regulatory relationships.

Each credit union retains its identity and its relationship with its members. But it doesn't have to build a core banking system from scratch. It plugs into infrastructure that the network maintains collectively.

What would the Muslim social services equivalent look like?

The Federated Stack

I've been developing a rough model I call the federated stack. The premise: there are layers of organizational infrastructure where local differentiation adds no value, and shared infrastructure would add enormous value.

These include:

  • Case management software — the underlying platform, not the workflows, which should remain locally configurable
  • Financial controls and reporting — back-office accounting, audit preparation, CRA compliance
  • HR infrastructure — onboarding, policy templates, benefits administration
  • Data and outcomes reporting — shared measurement frameworks that let funders compare across organizations

What should remain local: governance, programming decisions, client relationships, community identity, fundraising voice.

The Sovereignty Question

The pushback I hear most often is about autonomy. Organizations worry that federation means ceding control — to a larger body, to a dominant player in the network, to a vendor who builds the shared infrastructure.

This is a legitimate concern. Federation designed poorly can reproduce the same power imbalances it claims to solve. The credit union model has its own tensions around this.

But the alternative — every organization building everything independently — has a hidden cost that we've normalized. We've accepted fragility as the price of sovereignty. I think we can do better.
