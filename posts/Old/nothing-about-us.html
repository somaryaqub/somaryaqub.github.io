A Principle Under Pressure

"Nothing about us without us" began as a disability rights slogan. It has since migrated into the broader vocabulary of community-centred practice — social services, public health, urban planning, technology design.

Like many principles that travel widely, it has been diluted in transit. It now shows up in funding applications as a checkbox. In strategic plans as a value statement. In product roadmaps as a design methodology footnote.

What it rarely shows up as: an actual constraint on decision-making.

What the Principle Demands

Applied rigorously to case management software — tools used by thousands of social workers to manage information about hundreds of thousands of vulnerable people — the principle makes specific demands:

On design: People with lived experience of the services must be involved in designing the intake flows, the data fields, the language of the forms. Not consulted after design. Involved in design.

On data ownership: The person whose information is in the system should be able to see it, correct it, understand who has accessed it, and request its deletion. These aren't nice-to-haves. They're the operational expression of the principle.

On outcomes definition: The metrics that determine whether a program is "successful" should be defined, at least in part, by the people the program serves. A housing program that meets its occupancy targets but leaves residents feeling surveilled and controlled has not succeeded by the measure that matters most.

The Vendor Landscape

I've spent the last several years building and selling case management software to social service organizations. The honest assessment of where the vendor landscape sits on this principle: poorly.

Most systems are designed for the worker and the organization. The client is a record. A case number. A set of fields to be populated. The interface the client sees — if they see one at all — is typically a stripped-down afterthought appended to a worker-facing system.

This isn't because vendors are cynical. It's because the buyers are organizations, not clients. The procurement conversation is with executive directors and IT administrators, not with the people who will be entered into the system.

Building Differently

Transform, the platform I've built at Flourishing Systems, attempts to take this more seriously. Client-facing portals with plain-language summaries of their own records. Consent management that's legible, not buried in a terms-of-service. Outcome measurement that loops back to the client's own goals, not just the funder's indicators.

I won't pretend we've solved this. But treating "nothing about us without us" as an actual design constraint — rather than a marketing claim — changes the decisions you make at every level of the stack.

The principle is demanding. That's the point.
