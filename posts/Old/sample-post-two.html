A year ago we launched the People-First Data Charter with a quiet announcement and modest expectations. Twelve months later, the uptake has been uneven, the conversations it's sparked have been rich, and the resistance it's occasionally met has been clarifying.

Here's what a year has taught us.

Consent is not a checkbox

The most common feedback from organizations that adopted the charter: their existing consent processes were performative. Clients signed forms because forms were in front of them — not because they understood what they were agreeing to, or believed they had a real choice.

Meaningful consent requires plain language, time, and the genuine option to say no without consequence. Most intake processes aren't designed for that. Changing them is harder than signing a charter, but that's the point.

Trust is built in small moments

Several organizations told us that simply telling clients what data was being collected — and why — changed the texture of the intake relationship. Not a legal disclosure. A real conversation. "We're going to track whether you're still housed six months from now. Here's how we use that. Here's who sees it."

Clients responded with more candor, not less. Trust, it turns out, is a two-way street.

What's next

We're working on a second version of the charter that addresses aggregate data sharing — the practice of pooling anonymized data across agencies for sector-wide analysis. Done poorly, this re-identifies people. Done well, it produces insights that genuinely benefit communities. The line between the two is narrower than most assume.
