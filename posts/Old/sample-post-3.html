Grant applications have an epistemological problem. They ask for outcomes data — proof that programs work — but the systems that would generate that data are precisely what's underfunded. It's a catch-22 that punishes the organizations most in need of investment.

What Funders Are Actually Asking For

A typical outcomes-focused grant application asks an organization to demonstrate: who they served, what changed for those people, and how they know. Reasonable questions. But answering them rigorously requires a case management system, trained staff, consistent data collection protocols, and time for analysis.

All of those things cost money. And that money is not in the grant you're applying for — it's in the organizational capacity grant you didn't get because you didn't have outcomes data.

We want to fund what works. But we're not funding the systems that would tell you what works.

The Organizations Left Out

The organizations hit hardest by this dynamic are typically newer, smaller, or serving communities that haven't historically been legible to mainstream funders. They do real work. They have real impact. But they lack the infrastructure to prove it in terms funders recognize.

The organizations that thrive in this environment are often the ones with the longest funder relationships and the most administrative capacity — organizations that got funded before the current evidence standards were established, and have had decades to build the infrastructure that now makes them competitive.

What a Better Approach Looks Like

A few funders are beginning to take a different approach. They're investing in organizational infrastructure explicitly — funding the case management systems, the data analysts, the evaluation capacity — before asking for outcomes data. They're treating capacity as a precondition for evidence, not a reward for having it.

That's the right direction. The alternative — continuing to fund on the basis of data quality while refusing to fund data quality — is a system that reproduces existing inequities and calls it rigor.
