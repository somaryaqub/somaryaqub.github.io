Beehiiv arrived at the right moment. Substack had normalized the idea of the writer-as-publisher, but its union with controversy made some writers uneasy. Ghost was powerful but technical. Beehiiv threaded a needle: clean design, built-in monetization, and a growth toolkit that actually worked.

I've spent time with it. Here's my honest read.

What it gets right

The reading experience is excellent. Beehiiv newsletters land cleanly in inboxes and render beautifully on web. The typography choices are restrained and readable. They clearly thought about the person on the other end of the send button.

The analytics are honest — open rates, click rates, subscriber growth over time. Nothing gamified, nothing designed to make your numbers look better than they are. That's rarer than it should be.

The referral program mechanics are genuinely clever. Readers refer friends; friends become subscribers; subscribers unlock rewards. Growth compounding without paid acquisition. For the right writer, this works.

What it misses

Beehiiv is optimized for growth. Every feature nudges you toward more subscribers, more opens, more clicks. That's fine for some writers. But for others — those writing to a specific community, those prioritizing depth over reach — the platform's incentives quietly work against you.

There's also no meaningful answer to the question of data ownership. Your subscriber list lives in their system. If they change pricing, get acquired, or shut down, your most valuable asset is at risk. Newsletter platforms have a structural conflict of interest here, and Beehiiv hasn't resolved it.

For independent publishers serious about their work, owning your infrastructure — or at least understanding what you don't own — matters more than any growth feature.
