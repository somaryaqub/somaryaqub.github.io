The intake form is not neutral.

Every field we require of a vulnerable person — their immigration status, their mental health history, their household income — is a small act of power. Most of us in the social sector have never paused to interrogate it. We inherited the form from the organization that hired us, who inherited it from the funder who required it, who inherited it from a policy framework drafted in the 1990s.

The chain of custody on our data practices is almost entirely invisible to the communities we serve.

What funders actually want

Let me be honest about what's driving this. Funders want proof. Proof that the money is going somewhere, that "vulnerable populations" are being reached, that outcomes are being measured. This is reasonable. Stewardship of public and philanthropic dollars requires accountability.

But accountability to funders and accountability to communities are not the same thing — and we've spent thirty years building systems that serve the former while treating the latter as incidental.

What communities actually lose

A newcomer family sits across from a caseworker at an intake appointment. They are asked to disclose their legal status, their children's birthdates, their mental health diagnoses. They sign a consent form they may not fully understand, in a language that isn't their first.

That data goes into a case management system. It gets exported into a funder report. It might get shared with partner organizations under a data-sharing agreement the family never read. It might persist for seven years because that's the retention policy.

They signed the form. Were they really consenting?

What we can do

The People-First Data Charter — a framework I've been developing at IslamicFamily — starts with a simple premise: no data collection without a clear service benefit to the person being asked. Not a benefit to the organization. Not a benefit to the funder. To the person.

This sounds obvious. It is shockingly radical in practice.
