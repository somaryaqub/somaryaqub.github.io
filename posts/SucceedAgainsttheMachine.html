How to Succeed Against the Machine

For community leaders who are tired, and still here anyway.

✸ Introduction

You already know what the cycle looks like. Crisis. Deflection. Committees. Reports. Community voices become exhausted. Memories fade. And then it begins again.

The machine doesn't need to work to defeat you. It just needs you to give up. That's not pessimism; that's the design. Understanding it is the first step towards another future.

What follows are five principles for people who see success as skillfully striving and take the work of removing obstacles that face community seriously.

✸

Between Abolitionist & Apologist

Someone reading this from an abolitionist position might find the scope of this article insufficient. They’re right; more work needs to be done. This work serves  larger movements but doesn't substitute for them. Disagreements about destination don’t have to be a disagreement about urgency. Some of us want a transformed police service. Some want no police whatsoever. What we agree on is that unaccountable power causes harm, and that the work of reducing that harm is urgent regardless of where you believe it ends.

✸
1Root yourself.

Sacred Grounding

This work must be anchored in the sacred and the communal, or you will not survive. The machine is designed to hollow you out: to erode conviction through fatigue, to cleave communities, split friendships, and lead you to despair. The communities that sustain advocacy over years are not the ones with the most resources. They are the ones whose work flows from something that cannot be outlasted.

That grounding is different for different people: faith, culture, ancestry, obligation to the next generation. What matters is that it exists, and that the work flows from it. The Islamic tradition speaks of niyyah — intention — as the foundation of every act. Work begun for personal vindication or visibility corrodes. Work pursued for a collective good, directed toward justice as a sacred obligation, succeeds regardless of material outcomes.

Sacred activism is not passive spirituality dressed in political clothing. It is the understanding that justice is a divine attribute, and that demanding it is not a favour we are asking for. It is a claim we are making on what is already right.

Sheikh Dawud Walid — Toward Sacred Activism

This worldview also reframes what winning means. An individualized view of success asks: what did I achieve? A grounded one asks: did I pursue justice while acting justly? A collective view asks: did the community move forward? Soul-rooted work can absorb setbacks, share burdens, and outlast any single person's capacity.

The Islamic tradition calls us to sabr, often translated as patience, but we can also understand it as a principled persistence in the face of resistance. Sabr is not waiting. It is not resignation, nor acquiescence. It is the refusal to be moved from what is true by what is difficult.

Belonging & Acceptance / Insiders & Activists

To endure in this work, we have to be precise about where we seek legitimacy and belonging. If we anchor ourselves in the recognition of those in power, we will be continually destabilized by their conditional and fleeting acceptance. Legitimacy must come from the communities we come from and will return to: our aunties and uncles, our elders, our peers, our children. They are not an audience, but a trust. When we orient ourselves this way, we stop asking to belong in rooms not built for us, and act instead on behalf of people to whom we already belong.

 At its best, this is collective work: activists shift the Overton window, creating the conditions for organizers and insiders to move systems toward new possibilities. Those working within institutions, often as allies, are under real pressure, navigating difficult terrain where the line between principled compromise and quiet capitulation is not always visible from the outside.

There are moments when alignment fractures: when the work shifts from pushing for change to defending inaction, when a position becomes a shield for the institution rather than pressure on it and conversely when outside agitation pushes past what can be condoned. Our response should not be immediate condemnation. It is accountability delivered with love: calling one another back to the community that gave us standing, reminding each other where home is. The sacred lens here is not punitive but relational, the understanding that we are responsible for one another.

Hospitable Accountability

We succeed not by emulating oppressive tactics but by holding on to our values. Hospitality is part of this rootedness, not a vulnerability to be managed but a genuine cultural and moral value to be carried with intention. When someone in power walks through our doors, we meet them. We offer warmth. We extend good faith, not with naïveté or impunity but with hope. We can honour a guest and still ask what they'll do, by when, and what happened with previous commitments. Warmth and accountability are not opposites.

  • Why am I doing this? Who am I doing it for? Do I care enough about this to be following up when others have forgotten?
  • What would it mean to measure success by the health of the community rather than the response of the institution?
  • When a powerful person leaves our gathering having been warmly received, what do we have that we didn't have before they arrived?
  • Begin meetings, planning sessions, and difficult conversations by naming what grounds the work: faith, ancestry, obligation, community. Don't assume it; speak it.
  • Build space for rest, reflection, and the sacred into the rhythm of the work. Burnout is not a personal failing; it is a structural risk to be designed against.
  • When an ally inside an institution drifts, reach for them with relationship before reaching for public critique. Accountability with love comes first.

2Play to win.

Endurance & Strategy

Knowing how to win means knowing the game you're actually in. Institutions depend on you not knowing the rules: on procedural complexity being a deterrent, on community leaders not knowing which body has real authority versus advisory authority, when a budget decision can be challenged, or what the difference between a Commission recommendation and a Council directive means in practice. These are not technicalities. They are essential knowledge for meaningful change.

Knowing governance architecture allows us to focus our energy. In Edmonton: the Police Commission has oversight responsibility; budget authority flows loosely from City Council, and the province legislates the framework.

Knowing this leads to learning about another powerful and underused tool: holding institutions to their own stated commitments. EPS, the Commission, the City; they have all published frameworks, strategic plans, community engagement policies. Learn their language. Cite their documents. When you say "you committed to this in your 2023 strategic plan," you've changed the room in a way that outside demands alone cannot.

Studying the game means being clear-eyed about what wins are achievable, and being honest about that with yourself and your community. Insurmountable demands are not noble; they are strategically irresponsible. They exhaust movements, give institutions an easy out, and produce little. Small structural wins, accumulated and protected over time, change the landscape.

The question is always: what is reachable from here? That question is the hardest one in advocacy. It cannot be answered by moral conviction alone, nor by cynical assessment of the status quo alone. It lives in the space between what is and what must be. Finding it requires study, coalition, timing, and political imagination.

Three forces work against you.

i
Time
This work is not a sprint; it's a marathon. Institutional change moves slowly by design. Every delay (intentional or otherwise) preserves the status quo, whether anyone intended it or not. Every "we're reviewing it" is a clock running, waiting for you to lose focus, time out, or give up. The community members who are still in the room five years later, who are there when the institution finally runs out of excuses, are not the ones who burned brightest at the start. They are the ones who prepared themselves for distance.
ii
Attention: where it goes, and what depletes it
Crisis churn is the pattern in which communities move from emergency to emergency without resolution, accumulating grief and exhaustion faster than they can be processed. Each new crisis demands a response: statements, meetings, presence, and emotional labour that empties the tank. And because each crisis feels urgent, because it is urgent, the slower, structural work gets deferred. Relationships fray. Focus scatters. The institution, which never had to spend its own attention this way, benefits from attention deflection.

Part of the antidote is to look deeper. Most crises are not random. They are symptoms of the same underlying systems: under-regulated use of force, absent accountability structures, a governance architecture that insulates decision-makers from consequence. When you can name the root cause underneath crises, you do two things at once: you respond to the immediate, and you connect it to a sustained solution. The crisis becomes evidence. The emergency becomes data. You are no longer only reacting; you are building a case.
iii
Fracturing
The third force is the most painful to name: communities under pressure fracture. The stress of perpetual crisis turns inward. Grievances that should be directed at systems get redirected at allies, at people who share your condition but not your exact position. The machine benefits from this too. Every fight among people who have more in common with each other than with the institution is a gift to the institution. Guard against it, not by suppressing disagreement, but by keeping the analysis anchored to systems and structures rather than personalities and factions.

Wins are the result of study, coalition, timing, and political imagination.

  • How many people in our coalition are close to burnout right now, and do we have structures to catch them?
  • What accountability commitments are currently at risk of being buried by the latest news cycle?
  • Do we know precisely which body has the authority to grant what we're asking for, and are we directing our pressure there?
  • What is the smallest structural win available to us right now that would make the next demand easier to achieve?
  • Identify incremental wins. What does your first win look like? What small win can set the stage for more material improvements?
  • Build in rotation and training. Advocacy burnout is predictable. Distribute leadership so no one person becomes the movement.
  • Before responding to a new crisis, ask: what prior commitment does this moment risk burying, and how do we keep it visible?
  • Study. Understand how decisions get made, what is within remit, what is possible, what is probable. Align asks with stated commitments and what is actionable; this has the greatest chance of success and of building momentum.

3Build coalitions.

Solidarity

Find alliances, tenuous or otherwise, that advance shared interest. And protect them.

The machine is effective at isolating. One community with a complaint is an incident. Ten organizations with the same concern, coordinated, is a movement. The difference is not the merit of the argument, but the weight of it, and how much harder it becomes to ignore or dismiss.

Coalitions don't require unanimity. They require a shared intent on specific interests, even if only briefly. Communities that disagree on other matters can agree on accountability. Organizations with different mandates can sign the same letter, and reiterate the same talking points. Alliances can be purposeful and bounded without being permanent or unconditional.

Coalition building isn't just between communities; it is within as well. Coalitions must span generations. Elders carry relationships, credibility, and institutional memory. Younger members carry urgency, different networks, and a refusal to accept what older advocates have sometimes learned to live with. Both are essential. The tensions between them are evidence that the work is alive across time, and that diverse tactics can lead to common goals.

Institutions will attempt to pick community voices individually: to find a moderate representative, to offer a seat at a table, to fragment solidarity through selective inclusion. The answer to "we spoke to community leaders" is: which ones, and who decided they speak for us?

Coalitions need advance agreements, before the pressure comes, about how members will handle individual invitations, appointments, and public statements. Not as a loyalty oath, but as a shared understanding of what the coalition is and what it is working toward.

  • Are we bringing younger voices into leadership, or into attendance?
  • When someone in our coalition softens their position, do we have the relationships and the language to have that conversation with them, with accountability and with love?
  • Which alliances have we under-invested in because the relationship felt complicated, even though the shared interest was clear?
  • Name your coalition explicitly in every public communication. Lists of signatories communicate breadth that rhetoric alone cannot.
  • Build internal protocols, before the pressure arrives, for how members handle individual invitations, appointments, and media requests.
  • Fund your coalition structure independently. Organizations dependent on institutional goodwill cannot advocate freely.

4Keep receipts.

Institutional Memory

Documentation is not bureaucracy. It is the building blocks of effective mobilization. Institutions have paid staff whose job includes institutional memory. Your coalition has volunteers who are exhausted and overextended. This asymmetry is one of the machine's quietest advantages, and one of the most correctable.

When you can say "you committed to this in March 2023, in this letter, signed by this person," you have changed the room in a way that passion alone cannot. Documentation converts commitment into accountability. It closes the escape route of forgetfulness. It makes the cycle — the surface, deflect, proclaim, forget pattern — legible and nameable rather than deniable.

This also means holding institutions to their own paper trail. Published equity frameworks. Strategic plans. Community engagement policies. Minutes from their own meetings. When you cite an institution's own language back to them, it is very difficult to dismiss. You are not making an outside demand; you are asking them to be who they said they are.

The machine's greatest advantage is the institutional memory it controls and the community memory that fades. Keeping receipts is how you close that gap. It is not administrative work. It is the work.

Documentation also serves the long game in ways that aren't immediately visible. The pattern of deflection — recommendations declined, engagement promised but not delivered, commitments made and quietly abandoned — becomes visible only when it is recorded across time. Each individual incident can be explained away. The pattern cannot.

  • If a key person in our coalition left tomorrow, would we still have a complete record of every commitment made to us, every response received, every meeting held?
  • Are we reading the institution's own published commitments as carefully as we read their responses to ours?
  • What pattern across the last three years would become visible if we lined up the receipts side by side?
  • After every meeting with institutional power, send a written summary within 48 hours confirming what was discussed and any commitments made. Create the paper trail even when they won't.
  • Maintain a shared, accessible record of every commitment made to your coalition: dated, attributed, tracked against what actually happened.
  • Read the institution's own strategic plans, equity frameworks, and meeting minutes. Cite them. The gap between stated commitments and actual conduct is one of your strongest arguments.
  • Treat documentation as coalition infrastructure: assign it, resource it, protect it from being the first thing that falls away when capacity gets tight.

5Tell your own story.

Narrative Power

Every struggle is also a contest over meaning. Name the frame before anyone else does. The frame that gets established early tends to persist. Institutions invest heavily in naming things first: "Community consultation." "Concerns noted." "Ongoing dialogue." These are not neutral descriptions; they are frames that presuppose the legitimacy of process and defer the obligation of outcome. By the time the community pushes back, the frame has already done its work.

Community advocates must be equally intentional. The question is never "are you for or against the police?" It is "who has authority, who is accountable, and to whom?" That reframe moves the conversation from identity to governance. It is much harder to dismiss. It also protects the coalition: you are not taking sides in a community conflict; you are making a claim about institutional responsibility.

In the social media age, narrative control is more complex and more urgent. The algorithm rewards outrage and conflict. It flattens context. It circulates the most inflammatory fragment of your message, not the most accurate one. The message that reaches your coalition is not the same as the message that reaches the persuadable public, and both matter, but they require different language, different channels, and different timing. Echo chambers are real on all sides. Breaking through to people who are not already convinced requires meeting them where they are, not where you wish they were.

Resist the pressure to become purely reactive. A coalition that only responds to what the institution does cedes the narrative entirely. Name your own vision proactively. Not just what you're against, but what you're building toward. The world as it should be, made vivid and specific enough that people can picture themselves in it.

Disasters don’t arrive without warning. They emerge from policies that enabled them, and policies emerge from narratives that normalized them. Preventing disasters means changing policy — and changing policy means changing stories. Narrative shift is the most underinvested work in this movement, and the most transformative.

  • What frame does the institution use to describe what we’re asking for, and are we allowing that frame to stand unchallenged?
  • Are we spending more time responding to their agenda than advancing our own? What would it look like to change that ratio?
  • When our message is shared by someone who wasn’t in the room, what survives the sharing? Is that what we want to survive?

  • Articulate the world you’re building toward, not only the one you’re resisting. People follow vision more readily than grievance.

Every advance that we now take for granted was once dismissed as impossible. Every accountability mechanism that now exists was once the demand of people who were told they were being unreasonable, unrealistic, or divisive.

The institutions you are challenging are not immovable. They are slow-moving, risk-averse, and dependent on the belief that resistance will eventually tire. Your job is to make that bet a losing one, not through exhaustion, but through rootedness. Through strategy. Through the stubborn, principled, communal insistence that what is right does not expire.

This is hard work. It will take longer than it should. There will be setbacks that feel like defeats and quiet victories that few will know about. There will be people who fall away and new people who arrive. There will be moments when the horizon seems impossibly far, and moments when something shifts and you can see how far you have already come.

Success is not an outcome—which may or may not come—success is the discipline of striving well. In our tradition, removing an obstacle from a path is an act of charity and a branch of faith. This world is deliberately set with barriers; recognizing them and committing to their removal is our soulful purpose.
