Community Accountability

The Machinery of Inaction.
How EPS Outlasts Accountability.

On the pattern of disengagement from Edmonton Police Service that masquerades as community partnership — and why it persists.

The Context

Edmonton Police Service has no shortage of community engagement processes. Roundtables, advisory committees, listening sessions, consultations — the infrastructure of dialogue is well-developed. And yet, year after year, the same communities return with the same unresolved concerns, met with the same responses, cycling through the same motions. This is not a coincidence.

What follows is an attempt to name, plainly and precisely, the pattern that community organizations — particularly those representing indigenous, racialized, newcomer, and marginalized Edmontonians — have encountered in their relationship with the Edmonton Police Service (EPS). It is written not to assign bad faith to individuals, but to describe a structural dynamic that produces predictable outcomes regardless of who holds any particular role. Naming it is the first condition for changing it.

✸
The Pattern

What community organizations continue to encounter with EPS is not a series of isolated missteps — it is a recognizable and repeating pattern. Legitimate questions are received as threats. Documented concerns are reframed as niche or exceptional. Systemic problems are acknowledged only long enough for the news cycle to pass. When community partners press for accountability, they are not engaged — they are replaced, their seats filled with new faces while the substance of their concerns goes unaddressed.

EPS's energy flows not toward solving problems but toward protecting existing arrangements. This is not a failure of communication. It is a governance failure — and communities have become skilled at recognizing the difference between a table they are genuinely invited to and one they are seated at to provide the appearance of consultation.

“Communities have become skilled at recognizing the difference between a table they are genuinely invited to and one they are seated at to provide the appearance of consultation.”
The Structure of Meetings

Nowhere is this dynamic more visible than in the structure of commission and community council meetings. These meetings are routinely organized around the Force reporting to the community — updates on initiatives, announcements of programs, summaries of activity. The community is positioned as audience, not interlocutor.

This is a fundamental inversion of what accountability requires. Meetings between a police service and the communities it serves should be structured around two things: hearing what community members have to say, and following up on what has previously been asked for. When neither of those functions is built into the architecture of the meeting, the meeting itself becomes a mechanism for the appearance of engagement rather than its substance.

Commission members and community council representatives have direct authority over how these meetings are designed. Reforming that design — prioritizing community voice, tracking outstanding commitments, and requiring substantive responses — is one of the most concrete steps available to those who hold oversight responsibility.

The Cost

The cost of this pattern is not abstract. Volunteer advocates and grassroots organizers — who show up on evenings and weekends, who give what they cannot afford to give — find themselves in an unwinnable contest against an institution with inexhaustible time, salaried staff, and no structural pressure to resolve anything. Eventually, community leaders burn out. If they persist without outcomes they lose legitimacy with their communities. If they give up at least they have a community to return to, so they step back, and collectively we lose people invested in making things better.

Attrition is not an accident. It is the strategy. The status quo at EPS depends on communities being isolated, under-resourced, and willing to give up.

✸
Why It Persists

When issues do reach public attention, EPS immediately reframes them as community-specific — particular to one group, one incident, one moment. The media cycle runs its course. Attention moves on.

And then, inevitably, another community surfaces the same concern. But now it falls to a new set of leaders — again on volunteered time, again without institutional support — to step into the fray and attempt constructive engagement with an EPS leadership that is fully compensated, tactically experienced, and carefully calibrated to perform the theatre of engagement without taking steps that would constitute actual accountability or change.

EPS does not need to take action or even apologize. It only needs to outlast the media cycle. And it does — because the asymmetry is structural, not incidental.

“EPS does not need to take action or even apologize. It only needs to outlast the media cycle.”
What Was Forgotten

EPS exists to adapt. Not to endure. Not to defend. To serve changing communities with changing needs. When its primary energy shifts from that mission toward self-preservation, it has already failed the people it was built to protect.

The question is no longer whether that failure is happening. The question is who is willing to name it — and what those with the authority to act are prepared to do.

What We Are Asking For

This is directed at those with the authority and responsibility to break this cycle: the Edmonton Police Commission, the Justice Minister and secondarily to City Council, and community leaders.

  • Treat unimplemented community recommendations as open governance failures — not closed files — until meaningfully resolved.
  • Require time-bound, independently verified follow-through on commitments made in engagement processes, with public reporting on outcomes.
  • Resource community organizations so that engagement with EPS is not a contest of endurance.
  • Refuse the reframing of systemic concerns as community-specific incidents. Pattern recognition is a governance responsibility.
  • Hold EPS accountable not for the quality of its process, but for the substance of its outcomes.

Trust is not rebuilt through better communications or refreshed advisory committees. It is rebuilt through follow-through — the slow, unglamorous work of doing what was promised.

Edmonton's communities have done their part. They have shown up, documented, engaged, and returned — often at significant personal cost. The next move belongs to those in positions of authority. History will record whether they made it.
