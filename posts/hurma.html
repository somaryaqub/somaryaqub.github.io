FOUNDATIONS

How to use this manual

This single manual replaces our earlier separate drafts. It moves from belief to practice in four movements: Foundations (why we do this and how we think), Commitments (what we stand for), Procedures (how we do it, step by step), and a Casebook (how we reason through hard situations).

To avoid repeating ourselves, each idea lives in one place: the principles are stated once here; the commitments are brief and point to the procedure that carries them out; the procedures are the single home for operational detail; and the casebook applies all of it rather than restating it.

Scope & care

This manual discusses abuse, including of children and sexual violence, in general terms and without graphic detail. It supports policy and training; it is not legal or clinical advice. Adapt every provision to current Alberta law, our insurer’s requirements, and our privacy obligations, and have counsel review before adoption. Where there is immediate risk to a person, follow emergency and mandatory-reporting duties first.

FOUNDATIONS

Our foundation: ḥurma

Ḥurma is the divinely granted inviolability of the human person — the protection of every body, dignity, and honour in our spaces from abuse, assault, and exploitation. Naming it as our foundation establishes that safety is not a compliance afterthought but a sacred trust (amāna), and reframes safeguarding away from suspicion and toward dignity. Two commitments sit above everything else:

  • First, do no harm. A clinical ethic and an Islamic one: avert harm before pursuing benefit. When a situation exceeds someone’s competence, restraint and referral are the faithful response — not improvisation.
  • The dignity of the vulnerable is our responsibility, not theirs to defend. The person who arrives carrying trauma, shame, or fear never bears the duty to manage the relationship’s boundaries. That duty always sits with whoever holds authority.
FOUNDATIONS

Our four roles — and why the role changes the duty

IslamicFamily holds four roles at once. The same incident can call for different action depending on which we are in — naming the role first keeps us from over-reaching where we have no authority, or under-reaching where we hold real responsibility.

EmployerConvenorPartnerWelcomer / service provider
  • As an employer, we are responsible for our staff, volunteers, and leaders — which gives us the authority and the duty to set expectations, supervise, and investigate our own people.
  • As a convenor of gatherings and programs, we control the conditions of our spaces — who facilitates, how supervision works, what behaviour is welcome, on-site, off-site, and online.
  • As a partner to mosques and other organizations, we coordinate but do not control. In a shared space we can set our own conditions and support a person, but another organization’s personnel and process belong to them.
  • As a welcomer and service provider, our default is to include people who need us; our limit is safety, set through conditions rather than reflexive exclusion. We do not adjudicate disputes between the people we serve, but we keep our spaces safe.

Two directions of duty

Safeguarding points outward and inward. Most of this manual is about protecting the people we serve. But our duty of care also runs to our own team: keeping the workplace free of harassment between colleagues, holding power within the office responsibly, and keeping people physically safe when they work late, alone, or off-site. That inward duty is set out in Commitment 11 and Procedure A6, and it builds on our After-Hours & Off-Site Work policy rather than repeating it.

FOUNDATIONS

What we understand about harm

Nearly every failure to protect people traces back to a power imbalance the more powerful party did not recognize, name, and manage. Four understandings sit beneath everything that follows.

UNDERSTANDING 1

Authority distorts consent

People do not relate to someone with authority as equals, even when everyone believes the relationship is voluntary. Saying “no” carries a cost the powerful party may never feel. “They agreed” does not settle the question where authority is present.

UNDERSTANDING 2

Harm is usually a pattern, not an event

Abuse rarely begins with an obvious violation. It begins with grooming — small, defensible-looking boundary crossings (gifts, loans, secrecy, special access) that reveal a pattern only when connected. We watch for patterns, not just single incidents.

UNDERSTANDING 3

Trauma changes memory and behaviour

People may delay disclosure for years, return to the person who hurt them, or recount events with gaps. These are effects of trauma, not signs of dishonesty. Staff who misread them harm the very people they exist to help.

UNDERSTANDING 4

Institutions look away

The second harm often comes from the surrounding system — disbelief, shaming, pressure to stay silent to protect a reputation. Our reflex to protect our image is itself a risk this manual deliberately counteracts.

Eight guiding principles — how we act

How we think before we reach for a rule. Everything in Parts 1–3 is an application of these.

PRINCIPLE 1

Welcome is our default; safety is our limit

We welcome people because they need us and often have nowhere else to turn. The limit is not who someone is or what they did, but a real, assessed risk to others’ safety.

PRINCIPLE 2

Distinguish discomfort from risk

Others’ unease, disapproval, or knowledge of a past is not grounds to exclude. Observed behaviour or a substantiated, ongoing risk is. We act on risk, not reputation or rumour.

PRINCIPLE 3

Assess the person, not the label

“Accused,” “convicted,” “ex-offender” are labels, not risk assessments. We look at this person, this role, this space, this history — individually. A past act is not a present danger.

PRINCIPLE 4

Some risks are categorical

Most things are balanced case by case. Child safety is not. A person with a relevant history of harm to children is never placed with access to children. The two-adult rule is never waived.

PRINCIPLE 5

Hold belief and fairness together

We believe and support the person who comes forward, and we give the person of concern a fair process. Believing one is not pre-judging the other; both flow from ḥurma.

PRINCIPLE 6

Conditions before exclusion

We reach for the least-restrictive measure that keeps people safe — an agreement, a schedule, supervision, a role limit — before a ban. Exclusion is a last resort.

PRINCIPLE 7

Share on a need-to-know basis

We neither broadcast a person’s history or a complaint, nor withhold a safety-relevant fact from the few who must know it. Privacy and protection are balanced deliberately.

PRINCIPLE 8

Consistency through process; accessibility by design

People get the same careful response wherever they meet us — gathering, home visit, online — through standard procedures made low-barrier, trauma-informed, and accessible.

The reconciling idea

Read together, these resolve the central tension of our work: we are restorative and protective. Welcome, reintegration, and healing are real commitments — made credible, not contradicted, by clear limits. A community that cannot protect the vulnerable cannot truly welcome anyone.

FOUNDATIONS

Where this comes from

This manual synthesizes The Hurma Project Podcast (Dr. Ingrid Mattson & Mihad Fahmy) and anchors each procedure in bodies of practice tested in settings close to ours. We reference these to show alignment with mainstream practice, not to adopt any single external regime.

  • Professional social work ethics — NASW Code (sexual contact with current clients prohibited absolutely and extended to former clients, Standard 1.09; dual relationships, 1.06; privacy, 1.07; “once a client, always a client”), and the Canadian CASW Code and ACSW Standards of Practice that bind our registered social workers.
  • Trauma-informed care — SAMHSA’s four R’s and six principles (safety; trustworthiness & transparency; peer support; collaboration & mutuality; empowerment, voice & choice; cultural, historical & gender issues), the foundation used by sexual-assault services including AASAS in Alberta.
  • Faith-sector safeguarding — the Catholic Charter for the Protection of Children and Young People (Dallas Charter, 2002, rev. 2005/2011/2018) and VIRTUS; Praesidium accreditation standards for youth-serving organizations: safe-environment programs, screening, training, prompt response, removal pending investigation, an independent review board, victim assistance, annual audits.
  • Canadian abuse-prevention practice — Plan to Protect’s five moves (assess; write policy/procedure; screen & train; manage documentation; audit), and the Imagine Canada Standards Program (governance, finance, fundraising, staff, volunteers) we are pursuing.

PART ONE

Our commitments

What IslamicFamily stands for. Each commitment is carried out by the procedure noted beside it.

COMMITMENT 1

Clear roles & honest limits

Every trust role has defined limits and escalation points; we know the edge of our competence and refer across it rather than improvising.→ Procedures A1, A4, A5

COMMITMENT 2

Boundaries are ours to keep

The person in authority holds the boundary. No romantic or sexual conduct with anyone we serve, with a defined cooling-off period; dual relationships are disclosed and managed.→ Procedures A2, A3

COMMITMENT 3

Children are protected categorically

Two-adult/line-of-sight at all times; screening and training for youth work; no role with child access for anyone with a relevant history of harm to children; no hush-ups.→ Procedures A1, A2; Principle 4

COMMITMENT 4

We respond well to disclosures

Every staff member and volunteer can receive a disclosure safely — believe, attend to safety, be honest about confidentiality, route correctly, never re-harm.→ Procedure B1

COMMITMENT 5

We meet our legal duties

The duty to report suspected child abuse is personal and non-delegable; we cooperate with authorities and act on credible knowledge, not only formal complaints.→ Procedures B2, B3

COMMITMENT 6

We supervise and consult

Direct-service workers receive regular supervision and protected peer consultation; consulting is expected of strong practitioners, and we tend to staff wellbeing.→ Procedure A4

COMMITMENT 7

We investigate fairly & never bury reports

Allegations against our people get impartial, procedurally fair fact-finding; roles are separated; findings are not suppressed; the board owns oversight.→ Procedures C2, C3

COMMITMENT 8

Fairness for the accused, without impunity

Respondents get notice and a real chance to respond; outcomes are proportionate and applied regardless of status; reputation never shields misconduct.→ Procedures C1, C3, D2

COMMITMENT 9

Inclusion, dignity & access

We consult the people affected, build for accessibility, and treat exclusion from our spaces as a real injury to dignity, not a default.→ Procedures A5, E2; Principle 8

COMMITMENT 10

We protect people, not reputations

When safety and reputation conflict, we choose people; criticism of our own practice is loyalty to our mission; no one who raises a concern faces retaliation.→ Procedures B3, E2; Understanding 4

COMMITMENT 11

A safe, respectful workplace for our team

No harassment, violence, or bullying between colleagues; power within the team is held responsibly; and we keep people physically safe when they work late, alone, or off-site — with a reporting route that works even when the concern is about someone senior.→ Procedure A6; After-Hours & Off-Site Work policy

PART TWO

Our procedures

Fifteen standard operating procedures across five phases. Each carries an “Aligns with” note to recognized practice.

PHASE A

Prevent

Make abuse hard to start and easy to spot.

SOP A1Screening, selection & onboarding
Applies toAll staff, volunteers, students, contractors, boardTriggerAny new or renewing role with participant contact
  1. Define the position and its risk level before recruiting — contact, access to vulnerable people, supervision, whether minors are involved. Risk sets screening depth.
  2. Application, written role description & signed code-of-conduct agreement.
  3. At least two reference checks, contacted directly, on suitability to work with vulnerable people.
  4. Criminal-record and vulnerable-sector check appropriate to the role; re-screen on a fixed cycle and on role change.
  5. Interview for character and conduct, not status alone; probe boundaries, judgment, and power dynamics.
  6. Orientation before contact (code, boundaries, two-adult rule, disclosure response, reporting duties) and a probationary period with closer oversight.

Aligns with

  • Plan to Protect (screening, vulnerable-sector checks, documented onboarding); Dallas Charter/VIRTUS (screening + training before service); Imagine Canada (staff & volunteer management).

Records

Application, signed acknowledgment, reference notes, record-check confirmation & date, orientation log, re-screen schedule.

SOP A2Boundaries & the two-adult rule
Applies toAll program delivery — on-site, off-site, online
  1. Two-adult / line-of-sight rule. No program places one adult alone and unobserved with a minor or vulnerable participant. One-to-one mentoring happens in open, visible, supervised settings or with a second screened adult.
  2. Visibility by design — rooms with windows/open doors; home visits and off-site sessions planned, observable, and logged.
  3. Organizational communication channels only; no private one-to-one messaging with minors.
  4. Online sessions follow the same logic — co-facilitated or recorded where appropriate; never a private one-to-one video with a minor on a personal account.
  5. No physical discipline ever; personal gifts, loans, and money transfers prohibited or logged and approved.
  6. Transport or off-hours contact requires prior approval and a logged reason.

Aligns with

  • Plan to Protect & church safe-environment programs (“rule of two”); Praesidium (supervision, limits on one-to-one access).

Escalate the pattern, not the instance

  • Repeated bids for unobserved time, secrecy, special access, off-channel contact, gifts, or money — classic grooming vectors.
SOP A3Dual relationships & conflicts of interest
Applies toAll trust roles
  1. Disclose any second or anticipated relationship with a participant (family, friendship, business, romantic interest, shared role) to a supervisor promptly and in writing.
  2. Assess & decide — proceed with safeguards, add oversight, or reassign the participant.
  3. Romantic / sexual boundary. No romantic or sexual conduct, comments, or marriage proposals toward anyone currently served, with a defined cooling-off period afterward. IslamicFamily adopts a specific standard (e.g., a minimum of one year, with some prior relationships a permanent bar) and writes it down rather than leaving it to discretion.
  4. Name the hat in unavoidable overlaps; never let casual remarks carry organizational or religious authority.

Aligns with

  • NASW 1.06/1.09 & CASW/ACSW (dual relationships; absolute bar on sexual contact with current clients; worker always holds the boundary); APA 3.05; chaplaincy cooling-off norms (one year to perpetuity).
SOP A4Supervision & peer consultation
Applies toAll direct-service roles
  1. Scheduled, structured supervision with someone competent to oversee the work — cases, decisions, boundaries, and the worker’s own responses.
  2. Protected case consultation; consulting is expected of strong practitioners, never treated as weakness.
  3. Self-monitoring — workers name transference and counter-transference and raise it.
  4. Critical-incident debriefs & staff wellbeing — people cannot hold space for others while drowning in their own pain.

Aligns with

  • Social-work & clinical pastoral education supervision norms; SAMHSA TIC (peer support, collaboration, staff wellbeing).
SOP A5Training, certification & the referral directory
Applies toAll staff, volunteers, board — tailored by role
  1. Core curriculum for every trust role: abuse & grooming; boundaries & dual relationships; trauma literacy; the two-adult rule; receiving a disclosure; the duty to report.
  2. Role-specific modules — youth safeguarding, frontline disclosure response, investigation skills for leads, governance oversight for the board.
  3. Certify before contact, refresh annually, logged and tracked.
  4. Maintain a current referral directory (mental health, legal aid, child protection, family violence, sexual-assault services, crisis lines); support and referral happen together, not as an abrupt handoff.

Aligns with

  • VIRTUS / Plan to Protect (standardized, certified, refreshed training); AASAS First Responder to Sexual Assault and Abuse training.
SOP A6Inter-office conduct & workplace safety
Applies toAll staff, volunteers, contractors, interns, board — and the EDTriggerAlways on; and any concern about conduct or safety between colleagues

This procedure turns the duty inward. It complements — and does not replace — the detailed After-Hours & Off-Site Work policy, which governs solo work, two-person presence, visibility, check-ins, and transportation.

Respectful conduct between colleagues

  1. No harassment, violence, or bullying between colleagues — any single or repeated unwelcome conduct, comment, or action that a reasonable person would know causes offence or humiliation, or harms a worker’s health and safety. Sexual harassment and discrimination on a protected ground are included.
  2. Power within the team is held responsibly. Managers, supervisors, and the ED carry authority over colleagues; as with clients, the more senior person holds the boundary. Reasonable management of work (direction, feedback, performance) is not harassment.
  3. Relationships across a reporting line are disclosed and managed — a romantic or close personal relationship between a manager and someone they supervise is disclosed to HR/the ED, and the reporting line is changed to remove the conflict (mirrors SOP A3).
  4. Coworker boundaries — the same care with gifts, money, secrecy, and off-channel contact that applies with participants applies to power-imbalanced relationships among staff.

Practical ways to keep people physically safe

  1. After-hours & lone work follow the After-Hours & Off-Site Work policy — avoid being on-site or off-site alone with only one other person; the one / two / three-plus visibility rules; doors and lighting; departure times.
  2. Check in and check out. Anyone working late, alone, or off-site checks in with their direct manager and the Office Manager (or ED if unavailable) with purpose and expected window, and checks out on leaving — an effective means of communication for anyone working alone.
  3. A reachable remote contact is designated and aware whenever a single staff member is working off-site.
  4. Transportation protocol — do not transport clients/participants (use the taxi line); follow the gender and approval rules for volunteers and carpooling; no one is required to travel with others.
  5. “Would I be comfortable explaining this publicly?” If not, change the situation — the quick test from the After-Hours policy.

Raising a concern — including about someone senior

  1. Multiple routes, at least one of which bypasses the person’s manager. A concern about a manager goes to HR or the ED; a concern about the ED goes to the board chair or an independent committee member. Power at the top is never a dead end.
  2. Confidentiality, investigation & no retaliation — concerns are handled confidentially, investigated through the same fair process as any other matter (SOP C2–C3), and protected from reprisal.

Alberta legal duty (employer)

Under Alberta’s OHS Act and OHS Code Part 27, IslamicFamily must maintain a single, written violence and harassment prevention plan — with measures to control the hazard, procedures to inform workers, confidentiality safeguards, investigation procedures, and worker training — reviewed at least every three years (and after a triggering incident or workplace change). Harassment tied to a protected ground also engages the Alberta Human Rights Act. This manual’s inter-office provisions should be read as part of that plan; confirm wording with counsel.

Aligns with

  • Alberta OHS Act & Code Part 27 (consolidated violence & harassment prevention plan; working-alone communication); Alberta Human Rights Act (protected-ground harassment); IslamicFamily After-Hours & Off-Site Work policy; Imagine Canada (staff management).

Records

Violence & harassment prevention plan; reporting-line/relationship disclosures; after-hours check-in/out logs; workplace-harassment/violence complaint & investigation records; required incident report (kept available to OHS on demand).

PHASE B

Recognize & respond

Respond in ways that protect the person and meet our duties.

SOP B1Receiving a disclosure
Applies toEveryone — all are potential first respondersTriggerAnyone discloses abuse, assault, or a boundary violation
  1. Stay calm; receive without judgment. No shock, disbelief, anger, or blame; let them speak in their own words without interruption.
  2. Believe and affirm that coming forward was right; don’t test the story or ask why they waited — delayed, non-linear disclosure is normal.
  3. Address immediate safety first; if anyone is in danger, call 911.
  4. Be honest about confidentiality before they continue — including any duty to report. Never promise secrecy you cannot keep.
  5. Don’t investigate; support and route. You don’t need all the details.
  6. Offer choice and connection; within legal limits the person keeps control of next steps.
  7. Route & record factually in their own words; store securely (SOP E1).

Never say

  • “Are you sure?” / “He’s so respected.” · “Why now?” · “Let’s not cause discord / give seventy excuses.” · Anything that shifts responsibility onto the person harmed.

Aligns with

  • SAMHSA TIC (safety; trustworthiness; empowerment, voice & choice); AASAS / sexual-assault-centre survivor-centred response; Government of Alberta disclosure guidance.
SOP B2Mandatory reporting & duty to report
Applies toEveryone — the duty is personal & cannot be delegatedTriggerReasonable grounds to believe a child is being or may be harmed

Alberta legal duty

Under Alberta’s Child, Youth and Family Enhancement Act, any person with reasonable or probable grounds to believe a child is being — or is at risk of being — abused or neglected must report it promptly. You cannot rely on someone else, including a supervisor, to report for you, and failing to report carries penalties. You do not need proof; assessing the facts is the investigator’s job. Immediate danger → call 911 first.

  1. Immediate danger → 911.
  2. Report to the provincial Child Intervention intake line (or local Children’s Services / RCMP) without delay — yourself, if you hold the grounds.
  3. Tell the safeguarding lead in parallel; internal notice supports our response but never replaces or delays your personal legal report.
  4. For adults, respect their choice about police where the law allows; offer sexual-assault services and Alberta’s ONE LiNE; follow legal advice where a crime or ongoing risk to others is involved.
  5. Record the grounds, what/when, time, and report reference — factually.

Aligns with

  • Alberta Child, Youth and Family Enhancement Act (universal, non-delegable duty); Dallas Charter (cooperate with civil authorities); Plan to Protect (report & respond).
SOP B3Complaint intake & triage
Applies toConcerns about any staff, volunteer, leader, or board member
  1. Multiple channels — at least one bypasses a person’s direct supervisor and reaches the safeguarding lead or an independent committee member. We accept concerns in person/virtually (two staff), in writing (transcribed if needed), and via the helpline (780 900 2777) and feedback link.
  2. Knowledge triggers action — a formal complaint is not required.
  3. Triage within a set timeframe — log, screen for any mandatory report and immediate-safety need, classify (conduct/boundary vs. possible criminal matter).
  4. Anti-retaliation, in writing; acknowledge receipt and explain next steps and supports.

Aligns with

  • Workplace-investigation practice (action triggered by knowledge); Dallas Charter review-board model (independent oversight of allegations).
PHASE C

Investigate & decide

Find facts fairly, keep everyone safe meanwhile, decide proportionately.

SOP C1Interim safety measures
Applies toAny open allegation involving risk
  1. Assess risk immediately — to the person who came forward, to others, to the integrity of the process.
  2. Apply proportionate, precautionary measures (paused contact, reassignment, administrative leave) framed explicitly as non-disciplinary, not a finding of guilt.
  3. Protect the person who disclosed from contact or pressure; offer adjustments at their choice.
  4. Preserve evidence and instruct against destruction; document the rationale and review as facts develop.

Aligns with

  • Dallas Charter (removal from duty pending investigation); workplace-investigation practice (interim measures, evidence preservation).
SOP C2Conducting or commissioning an investigation
Applies toSubstantiable allegations against anyone acting for IslamicFamily
  1. Choose a trained, impartial investigator — external for serious or sensitive matters; independent of the parties and of day-to-day management.
  2. Define scope & process in writing, and confirm in advance how the report will be handled — it will not be buried.
  3. Meet the complainant for the specific particulars (who, what, when, where).
  4. Give the respondent the allegations in writing and a genuine opportunity to respond — procedural fairness, not a courtesy.
  5. Interview witnesses individually; gather documentary evidence. A support person may accompany parties.
  6. Make findings on the balance of probabilities. This is fact-finding — not a trial (no cross-examination) and not a mediation.
  7. Deliver the report per the pre-agreed handling, with appropriate sharing to the parties.

Guard against both failures equally

  • Burying a credible report to protect a respondent or our image; and railroading a respondent without notice or a real chance to respond.

Aligns with

  • Workplace-investigation practice (Fahmy & Bhabha, Hurma Project) — particulars, written allegations, individual interviews, findings on fairness principles, reports not buried, board liability; Dallas Charter (prompt response).
SOP C3Findings, decisions & proportionate response
Applies toCompleted investigations
  1. Separate the roles — supporting the complainant, investigating, and deciding are not done by the same people.
  2. Match response to conduct — predatory abuse (removal + reporting) vs. boundary errors from inadequate training (education, supervision, final warning) — never minimizing genuine harm.
  3. Honour legal duties — where findings indicate a crime or ongoing risk, ensure authorities are notified regardless of the internal outcome.
  4. Communicate with care — inform parties appropriately and lawfully; factual, non-inflammatory; protect privacy.
  5. Capture learning for review & audit (SOP E2).

Aligns with

  • Dallas Charter (discipline, accountability, prevention; zero tolerance for substantiated abuse); employment & human-rights proportionality.
PHASE D

Support & heal

Center the dignity of the person harmed; treat respondents fairly.

SOP D1Supporting the person harmed
Applies toAnyone who discloses harm
  1. Offer a consistent support person independent of the investigation.
  2. Co-create a support plan — safety, practical needs, specialist referrals, program accommodations — led by the person’s own choices.
  3. Keep them informed without making them carry the process.
  4. Counter shame and isolation with acceptance — the conditions under which healing, and even post-traumatic growth, become possible.
  5. Follow up after the matter closes.

Aligns with

  • SAMHSA TIC (empowerment; resisting re-traumatization); AASAS / victim-assistance practice; Dallas Charter (victim assistance, healing).
SOP D2Working fairly with respondents
Applies toStaff/volunteer respondents; participants who are themselves accused
  1. Notice and voice — the allegations in writing and a real chance to respond before findings.
  2. Presumption pending findings — interim measures are precautionary, not punitive; dignity throughout.
  3. Refuse the misuse of fairness — reputation, piety, usefulness, or “seventy excuses” never shut down legitimate scrutiny.
  4. Proportionate, consistent outcomes regardless of seniority or status.
  5. Support where appropriate — for participants we serve who are accused of crimes, continue rights-respecting support while honouring safety and reporting duties.

Aligns with

  • Procedural fairness (human-rights & workplace practice); the Hurma Project (belief is not pre-judgment; most who err are fallible, not predators — so fair process matters).
PHASE E

Govern & improve

Keep records right, protect privacy, get better every year.

SOP E1Records, documentation & privacy
Applies toAll safeguarding records
  1. Document factually and promptly — in the person’s own words where possible, dated, without speculation.
  2. Restrict access on a need-to-know basis; store securely (encrypted, access-controlled).
  3. Apply privacy law — in Alberta, private-sector privacy law as applicable, and health-information law where it applies; collect only what is necessary; honour the confidentiality limits disclosed at intake.
  4. Set retention & disposal schedules; never alter or destroy records tied to an open or foreseeable matter.

Aligns with

  • NASW 1.07 / social-work documentation standards; Plan to Protect (documentation management); Alberta privacy law.
SOP E2Review, audit & continuous improvement
Applies toBoard, safeguarding lead, ethics/safeguarding committee
  1. Annual policy & procedure review, with input from staff and the people served — consult the affected, especially groups whose experience leaders may not see.
  2. Audit compliance — screening currency, training completion, two-adult-rule adherence, complaint handling, record security — via gap analysis.
  3. Review every incident for systemic lessons; feed them back into prevention.
  4. Report to the board annually on safeguarding status and incidents (de-identified) — a governance responsibility, not delegated away.

Aligns with

  • Dallas Charter (annual audits, review board); Plan to Protect (audits, gap analysis); Imagine Canada Standards; integrates with our Quality Improvement Policy.
PART THREE

Casebook

The principles and procedures applied to situations we actually face. Each scenario points back rather than restating.

SCENARIO 1Welcoming someone accused — or convicted — of a crime
WelcomerConvenor

A person charged with, or convicted of, an offence wants to take part in our programs. Some staff or participants know and are uncomfortable — or afraid. How do we honour welcome, reintegration, and healing while keeping our spaces safe?

Separate three questions

  • Charged vs. convicted. Someone merely accused is presumed innocent; we don’t pre-punish on a charge or rumour, though we may take precautionary safety steps where there is real risk.
  • Discomfort vs. risk (Principles 1–2). That others know or disapprove is not grounds to exclude; an assessed, ongoing risk to specific people is.
  • What role/setting? Greeting adults at a meal differs from mentoring youth. Where the history involves harm to children, the answer is categorical (Principle 4): no access to children, ever.

What we do

  1. Default to welcome (Principle 1) for general participation, absent a real safety reason.
  2. Make an individualized risk assessment (Principle 3) with the safeguarding lead — nature/recency of conduct, any orders in force, who shares the space, the role sought.
  3. Manage with conditions before exclusion (Principle 6) — participation agreement, supervised attendance, role limits, non-overlapping scheduling.
  4. Honour any court order; align with the Handling Harassment Claims approach if another participant is the protected person.
  5. Address discomfort honestly and privately without disclosing the person’s history beyond need-to-know (Principle 7).
  6. Offer support to all sides — integration and healing for the person returning; reassurance and safety planning for anyone who feels unsafe.

What we don’t do

  • Exclude solely because of discomfort or unproven rumour; pre-punish a charge as if a conviction; ever place someone with a relevant history of harming children near children; broadcast a record; or take on adjudicating guilt.

Connects to

  • Commitments 3, 8, 9 · Procedures C1, D2, A2 · Companion policies: Handling Harassment Claims; Denying Service/Removal From Programming.
SCENARIO 2Consistent, accessible procedures for gatherings & outreach
ConvenorWelcomerPartner

We run gatherings, programs, outreach, and home-based support across many sites — our spaces, partner mosques, schools, homes, online. How do people get the same safe, welcoming experience everywhere, without it feeling like a checkpoint?

What we do — one backbone, every setting (Principle 8)

  1. Plan safety before the event — a short standard check: facilitators screened (A1), the two-adult arrangement, accessibility, the named event safety lead, and how to raise a concern.
  2. Name an event safety lead responsible for the space and the first point of contact.
  3. Post conduct expectations and how to get help in plain language — including the helpline (780 900 2777) and feedback link.
  4. Build for access — physical accessibility, interpretation/plain language, gender-considerate arrangements, childcare where possible, low/no cost, a welcoming tone.
  5. Same rules off-site and online (SOP A2) — home visits planned and observable; one-to-one with minors never unobserved.
  6. Coordinate in partner spaces — agree who holds the safety lead, whose rules apply, and how a concern about the host’s personnel is handled (Scenario 4).
  7. If something happens, the safety lead follows SOP B1, screens for a mandatory report (B2), takes any immediate step, and records it.
  8. Handle photography and consent deliberately — some people we serve have real safety reasons not to be photographed; ask, offer opt-outs, default to caution.

Make it a one-page checklist

Turn this backbone into a single pre-event safety checklist any facilitator completes — the same form for a 200-person gathering, a youth night, or a home visit.

Connects to

  • Commitments 3, 6, 9 · Procedures A1, A2, A5, B1–B2, E1.
SCENARIO 3Hiring a candidate with a criminal record or a flagged check
Employer

A strong candidate discloses a record, or a vulnerable-sector check comes back flagged. We believe in second chances — and we are responsible for the safety of those we serve. How do we decide?

The test is fit between the record and the role

  • Relevance — does the conduct bear on this role and the people it serves? (A relevant offence against children rules out any role with child access — Principle 4.)
  • Recency, pattern, circumstances, rehabilitation.
  • Mitigation through conditions (Principle 6) — can supervision or role limits manage the risk short of exclusion?

In Alberta, a criminal record is generally not a protected ground under the Alberta Human Rights Act, so we have latitude to consider it — but the right tool is individualized, role-based assessment, not a blanket bar (which can screen out good people and have an unfair impact on protected groups). The federal Act protects pardoned / record-suspended convictions.

What we do

  1. Match screening to role risk (A1) — vulnerable-sector checks for child/vulnerable-adult access.
  2. Assess individually, documenting relevance, recency, mitigation, and reasoning.
  3. Use conditions where safe rather than a blanket no; apply the categorical child rule absolutely.
  4. Handle the information privately (Principle 7) and confirm approach with counsel.

What we don’t do

  • Apply a blanket “no record ever” rule; ignore a safety-relevant record out of generosity; or treat a pardoned/record-suspended conviction as if it still exists where the law protects it.

Connects to

  • Commitments 1, 3 · Procedures A1, E1 · Confirm with counsel: Alberta Human Rights Act vs. federal protections.
SCENARIO 4A concern about someone at a partner mosque or organization
PartnerWelcomer

During a program we co-host at a partner mosque, a participant says that an imam, volunteer, or staff member of the partner — not of IslamicFamily — made them unsafe. The person of concern is not ours. What can we do?

What we do

  1. Respond to the person first (B1) — receive, believe, support, be honest about confidentiality.
  2. Meet legal duties ourselves (B2) — if a child is involved, the mandatory-report duty applies no matter whose personnel is implicated; we don’t defer it.
  3. Protect our shared space — adjust arrangements, add supervision, or pause the activity.
  4. Refer to the partner’s own process for their personnel and support a proper response — the outcome is theirs to own.
  5. Reassess the partnership if a partner won’t respond responsibly; safeguarding alignment is a condition of partnering.

What we don’t do

  • Investigate or discipline another organization’s staff as if they were ours; treat “not our employee” as “not our problem”; or stay silent to protect a partnership.

Connects to

  • Commitments 4, 5, 10 · Procedures B1, B2, C1.
SCENARIO 5A staff member and a participant develop feelings
EmployerWelcomer

A case worker and a participant they support have grown close, and a mutual romantic interest has developed. Both are adults; both say it is genuine. In a small community this is not rare. Is it a problem?

The issue is not the sincerity of anyone’s feelings; it is the power difference. A participant depending on a worker’s help cannot relate to them as an equal — so the boundary is the worker’s responsibility, and “they consented” does not resolve it where authority is present.

What we do

  1. The worker discloses to a supervisor promptly (A3).
  2. End the helping relationship cleanly — reassign the participant so the authority dynamic is removed.
  3. Apply the adopted cooling-off standard before any romantic relationship is appropriate; write the decision down.
  4. Support both people; the participant’s care continues uninterrupted.

What we don’t do

  • Treat “both adults, both consented” as the end of it; let the same worker keep supporting the participant while pursuing a relationship; or shame either person.

Connects to

  • Commitment 2 · Procedure A3.
SCENARIO 6A participant discloses ongoing harm during a program
Welcomer

Mid-program, a participant quietly tells a facilitator they are being abused at home — or that someone is exploiting them. The facilitator is not a clinician or a lawyer. What now?

What we do

  1. Receive without judgment (B1) — calm, believe, own words, address immediate safety.
  2. Be honest about confidentiality before they continue — including any duty to report.
  3. For a child: follow the mandatory-report duty (B2) — personal, non-delegable.
  4. For an adult: respect their choices where the law allows; offer specialist support and stay alongside.
  5. Don’t improvise expertise — refer to clinical/legal/specialist services (Commitment 1).
  6. Record factually and store securely (E1).

What we don’t do

  • Promise total secrecy then break it; interrogate or test the story; or try to handle a clinical or legal crisis ourselves instead of referring.

Connects to

  • Commitments 1, 4, 5 · Procedures B1, B2, A5.
SCENARIO 7Two people who need to be kept apart both want to attend
WelcomerConvenor

Two participants — e.g., parties to family violence, or with a protective order between them — both rely on our programs. Both have a right to be here. How do both keep getting help safely?

This is the territory of the existing Handling Harassment Claims Between Clients/Participants policy, which we follow. We don’t adjudicate the dispute, but we shape our space so neither has to choose between safety and access — and the person who feels unsafe is never required to face the other.

What we do

  1. Listen and co-identify options; the person who feels unsafe leads the choices.
  2. Non-overlapping scheduling or separate sessions so both can access services without contact.
  3. Assign separate staff to each party where possible.
  4. Enforce any active court order; support legal options and a restraining order where appropriate.
  5. Escalate to denial of service only if needed (Denying Service/Removal From Programming) — e.g., non-compliance or identified predatory intent.

Connects to

  • Companion policies: Handling Harassment Claims; Denying Service/Removal From Programming · Commitment 9 · Procedure D1.
SCENARIO 8A person who has been denied service keeps returning
ConvenorPartner

Someone asked not to attend — after a fair process — keeps showing up, including at an event we co-host at a partner mosque. Staff feel unsure and unsafe. What do we do?

What we do

  1. Keep staff and participants safe first — de-escalate; call 911 if there is any threat.
  2. The event safety lead handles it calmly and privately, reiterating the decision without re-litigating it.
  3. Coordinate with the partner in advance for co-hosted events — who enforces, how, and what their rules require in their space.
  4. Document each occurrence; review whether further steps (trespass, legal advice) are warranted.
  5. Leave a door for review — a denial is not necessarily forever (Principle 6).

What we don’t do

  • Let one staff member confront the person alone; assume a partner will enforce our decision in their space; or treat a denial as permanent without telling the person how it could be reviewed.

Connects to

  • Companion policy: Denying Service/Removal From Programming · Procedures C1, C3.
SCENARIO 9Harassment, or a power-imbalanced relationship, between colleagues
Employer

A staff member experiences unwelcome conduct from a colleague — comments, pressure, or bullying. In a variation, a manager and someone they directly supervise begin a romantic relationship. Sometimes the person of concern is senior, even the ED. What do we do?

The lens we use with clients applies inside the office too: the issue is conduct and power, not personalities. Where there is a reporting relationship, “it’s mutual” does not resolve the imbalance — the more senior person holds the boundary. And a concern about someone senior must have somewhere to go.

What we do

  1. Receive the concern (SOP A6) through a route that bypasses the person of concern — HR/ED for a manager; the board chair or an independent member for a concern about the ED.
  2. For a relationship across a reporting line: disclose it and change the reporting line to remove the conflict.
  3. Investigate fairly (C2–C3) — confidential, impartial, proportionate, with no retaliation — and meet the OHS Part 27 duty to investigate and document.
  4. Support everyone involved and protect the person who raised it from reprisal.

What we don’t do

  • Treat “reasonable management” (direction, feedback, performance) as harassment; let a relationship continue inside a reporting line; or let seniority make a concern a dead end.

Connects to

  • Commitments 2, 8, 11 · Procedures A6, A3, C2–C3 · Alberta OHS Code Part 27; Alberta Human Rights Act.
SCENARIO 10Working late, alone, or off-site
EmployerConvenor

A staff member needs to finish work in the office in the evening, run an after-hours program, or carry out an off-site visit alone. How do we keep them safe — both physically, and from situations that could be misread?

This is exactly what the After-Hours & Off-Site Work policy is for. The casebook’s job is only to point to it and reinforce the habit.

What we do

  1. Avoid being alone with only one other person after hours; if two people are present, use the open-door, common-area visibility rules.
  2. Check in with the direct manager and Office Manager (or ED) with purpose and expected window; check out on leaving; keep a reachable remote contact for solo off-site work.
  3. Follow the space rules — doors, lighting, locked exterior doors when truly alone, reasonable departure times.
  4. Follow the transportation protocol — no transporting participants; gender and approval rules for others; travel-partner choice respected.
  5. Use the public test — if you wouldn’t be comfortable explaining the situation publicly, change it.

Connects to

  • Commitment 11 · Procedure A6 · After-Hours & Off-Site Work policy (and its event checklist) · Alberta OHS working-alone requirements.

APPENDIX V

Adoption checklist

A sequence for moving from this manual to live practice.

  • Board approves the ḥurma foundation, the four-role framing, and this manual.
  • Name a safeguarding lead and stand up an ethics/safeguarding committee with an independent member.
  • Adopt the code of conduct and set our specific cooling-off standard (Commitment 2 / SOP A3).
  • Stand up screening (A1), the two-adult rule (A2), supervision (A4), and the training curriculum + referral directory (A5).
  • Publish the disclosure protocol (B1), mandatory-reporting duty (B2), and a multi-channel, anti-retaliation complaint route (B3).
  • Adopt the investigation procedure and the rule against burying reports (C2–C3).
  • Build the forms in Appendix II and the pre-event safety checklist.
  • Confirm insurance/liability coverage and privacy handling with counsel.
  • Set the annual review & audit (E2) and report safeguarding to the board.
