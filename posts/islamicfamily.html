Below are the constraints any good vision, mission, or values statement must respect. Keep them in mind as you review each option — and use the flag tool on any card that you think falls short of one.
Guardrail 1
Accessible to all
The language must not exclude anyone seeking services — Muslim or not, newcomer or fifth-generation Canadian. Insider framing or community-only phrasing closes doors before they can be opened.
Guardrail 2
Anchored in tradition
We are grounded in Islamic values — rahma, karama, adl, amanah. The language should reflect that rootedness without requiring it as a condition of access or belonging.
Guardrail 3
Breadth of community
Our community spans an enormous range of need and background: from pre-arrival refugees navigating displacement to fifth-generation Canadians struggling with identity and mental health. The language must hold that full spectrum without collapsing it.
Guardrail 4
Legible to outsiders
Funders, government partners, and referring agencies need to understand who we are without a translator. Avoid insider language, religious terminology without context, or framing that only makes sense from inside the community.
Guardrail 5
Honest about scope
We should not overclaim. The mission shouldn't promise what IslamicFamily alone cannot deliver. Aspirational language is essential — but overreach is a governance and credibility risk with funders and partners.
Guardrail 6
Durable, not dated
The language should hold up in ten years. Avoid references to current events, specific programs, or demographic trends that may shift. A good mission statement outlasts the strategic cycle that produced it.
Guardrail 7
Distinctive, not generic
"Compassionate whole-person care" could describe dozens of organizations. The language should actually sound like IslamicFamily — specific enough that it could not easily belong to anyone else.

Our values
How we show up
in everything we do
Every healthy organization has two kinds of values. Tag each value below as Core (who we fundamentally are) or Operational (how we do our work). Both matter — but they serve different purposes.
Core Values
Who we are
Core values define organizational identity — the non-negotiables that remain constant regardless of program, partner, or context. They answer: what makes IslamicFamily, IslamicFamily? Even if your work changed entirely, these would not.
Operational Values
How we work
Operational values guide daily decisions and team culture — they shape how we deliver on our identity. They may evolve as the organization grows and professionalizes. Both types are essential; conflating them leads to lists too long to remember.
Why this matters for IslamicFamily: As we grow toward multi-city delivery and deeper institutional health, our Core values must be stable enough to travel — recognized in Halifax or Calgary as much as Edmonton. Operational values can adapt to context.

Strategic Plan 1450
People, Impact
& Regeneration
Three tracks convert IslamicFamily's values into durable outcomes. Read what each track is trying to accomplish, then share your reflection.
🌱
Track 1
Nurture People
IslamicFamily has long run on the dedication of a few people carrying too much. This track is about changing that. It means investing in staff — real career ladders, better compensation, manageable caseloads — so that the people doing this work can sustain it. It also means building outward: strengthening the capacity of the broader Muslim organizational ecosystem so that we aren't the only place carrying this weight. The shift is from heroic effort to institutional health. People who flourish here help other people flourish.

📊
Track 2
Meaningful, Quantifiable Impact
For too long, success in this sector has been measured by activity — how many hampers distributed, how many sessions held, how many people through the door. This track is about a different question: did people's lives actually change? IslamicFamily wants to build the evidence infrastructure to answer that honestly — tracking housing stability, mental health, economic mobility, belonging — not just for funders, but because it makes the work better. Measuring well also means telling the story well: turning data into the case for why this community deserves more.
Your reflection — Impact

♻️
Track 3
Regenerative Growth
The nonprofit starvation cycle — chronically underfunding infrastructure to look lean — quietly breaks organizations. Regenerative growth is the alternative: building financial foundations strong enough to absorb shocks, invest in people, and actively generate community wealth rather than just sustaining existing programs. For IslamicFamily that means diversified revenue, multi-year commitments, a growing donor base, and aligning spending with the halal economy. It also means the Halal Housing moonshot — moving from advocacy to ownership, building the kind of community infrastructure that lasts generations.
