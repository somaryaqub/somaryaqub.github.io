Reentry That Works: A Scalable, Community-Driven Model for Reducing Recidivism Among Muslim Canadians

An institutionally anchored, research-integrated model for community-delivered reintegration — Prairie Region to national scale
ToAnne-Charlotte Ogbechi, Head of Chaplaincy and Community Reintegration, CSC
DateMarch 31, 2026
Primary Ask

That CSC support a five-year, $3.05M multi-year pilot with IslamicFamily and national partners to deliver, research, and scale a community-driven reentry framework for Muslim Canadians — beginning in the Prairie and Ontario regions and building toward full national coverage.

The Problem

A growing population, a widening gap

Canada's Muslim population has more than doubled in 20 years, rising from 2.0% of the general population in 2001 to 4.9% in 2021. That growth has not been matched by investment in culturally appropriate supports within the justice system.

Muslims now account for over 9% of the federal prison population — nearly double their share of the general population, and a proportion that has reached an all-time high. IslamicFamily field data suggests the true figure is higher still, at 11–13% in some Prairie Region facilities.

Sources: Statistics Canada Census of Population 2021; Investigative Journalism Foundation / CSC data, November 2023; IslamicFamily field observations.

Charter rights and service access

The disparity is not only numerical. Across federal institutions, Muslim individuals face documented access failures: Quran access restricted to English audio only in some facilities, halal diet unavailable in BC facilities as of 2026, chaplain visits as infrequent as once per month, and inconsistent access to funeral rites and prayer accommodations. More than 60% of incarcerated Muslim individuals engaged by IslamicFamily reported experiences of direct racism that violated basic Charter rights.

Inconsistency across institutions

There is no national standard for what Muslim religious accommodation or reentry support looks like inside federal corrections. What is available in one institution is often absent in the next. Without standardization, outcomes vary dramatically by facility — and by the individual relationships that happen to exist at a given site.

Community-side stigma: the undiscussed barrier

The reintegration challenge does not end at the institution gate. Families and mosques are often unprepared — or actively unwilling — to receive returning individuals. Stigma within the Muslim community itself compounds every other barrier, yet it is absent from most existing literature and unaddressed by existing programming. Any serious reentry model must treat community readiness as a core program component, not an afterthought.

Muslims are 4.9% of Canada's population and over 9% of federal prisoners. The gap is not accidental — and it is not self-correcting. It reflects a system that has grown without the culturally grounded infrastructure needed to reduce incarceration and enable successful reintegration.

The Challenge

Why this cannot be solved at the municipal level

The Fragmentation Trap

Muslim communities in Canada have crossed the threshold of visibility — large enough to generate complex, specialized need — but have not yet reached the density at the municipal level that would allow niche programming to be self-sustaining. Programs launch on thin funding, hire one or two people, and those people become generalists by necessity: absorbing counselling, systems navigation, crisis support, and advocacy because there is no one else. They cannot specialize, cannot scale, and cannot build the evidence base that would justify more resources. The program either flatlines or burns out its staff. This is the Fragmentation Trap.

The answer is not more small programs. It is fewer, larger ones — built at the regional or national level — that achieve sustainability through cost distribution across a broader base. A counselling team that serves corrections clients also serves survivors of domestic violence. A livelihoods specialist who supports returning individuals also supports other vulnerable populations. Shared infrastructure, dedicated specialists, distributed cost.

No national standards or continuity

Fragmented, city-by-city delivery cannot produce the national standards, shared referral protocols, or coordinated case management that effective reentry requires. There is no mechanism for institutions to hand off to communities, and no mechanism for communities to signal needs back to institutions. The custody-to-community boundary is a gap, not a bridge.

Institutional fragility: the individual relationship problem

When a partnership lives in a person rather than an organization, CSC loses its community partner the moment that person moves on. IslamicFamily experienced this directly: when its primary institutional relationship holder departed in 2025, months of rebuilding were required, with multiple CSC contacts initiating outreach to re-establish the connection. This is not an edge case — it is the predictable consequence of partnership models that rely on individual relationships rather than institutional infrastructure.

A mature partnership requires MOUs, standardized referral protocols, and shared program frameworks — not personal relationships that dissolve with staff turnover on either side. This proposal is designed to build exactly that.

Federal Precedent

The FCRP and D-FCRP: what has been proven, and what remains unbuilt

The Faith Community Reintegration Project (FCRP) establishes the policy foundation: CSC has formally recognized that faith-based, community-delivered reintegration programming is effective and worth funding. This proposal builds on that recognition — it does not ask CSC to validate a new concept.

The Directed FCRP (D-FCRP) is the operationalized, contracted stream. It is the natural funding pathway for this proposal. The current D-FCRP reality, however, is instructive: a single contracted organization operating in the Greater Toronto Area, delivering a mosque-first model with approximately $281,000 over three years, no wraparound social services, no research or evaluation strand, and no presence in five of CSC's six regions.

The gap is structural, not incidental. The D-FCRP has proven the concept but has not been built for national scale, multi-region delivery, or the full complexity of wraparound need. IslamicFamily is not proposing to replicate the GTA model — it is proposing to build what the D-FCRP pointed toward but has not yet reached.

The Opportunity

An integrated national model: three strands

IslamicFamily's proposed framework integrates service delivery, research, and policy into a single coordinated model — designed from the outset to be nationally portable and institutionally durable.

Strand 01

Direct Service Delivery and Community Reintegration

From the first in-reach visit to long after release, IslamicFamily provides the continuity of care that no other model currently offers — inside the institution, at the gate, and in the community the person returns to.

Continuity of care: bridging the gap

  • Conventional chaplaincy ends when people are released. There is currently no system to provide continuity of service across the custody-to-community boundary. This program is designed to be that system — with formalized handoff protocols ensuring every participant is connected to community supports before their release date
  • Consistent, scheduled in-reach programming in Prairie and Ontario region federal institutions — religious mentorship, individual needs assessments, and group programming that begins building the reintegration plan from day one of engagement
  • Wraparound reentry: culturally specific case management, trauma-informed counselling addressing grief, addiction, family separation, and identity, with direct referrals to livelihoods, housing, and legal supports

Community reintegration: the harder problem

  • For many participants, the same community ties that contributed to incarceration are now the primary reintegration resource — but those ties are strained, stigmatized, or geographically severed. This program treats community readiness as a core program component, not an afterthought
  • Reentry frequently happens far from the place of incarceration. A person incarcerated in Edmonton may return to Winnipeg, Calgary, or Toronto. City-level organizations cannot serve that. A national network — with trained community institutions across regions — is the only viable solution
  • Structured training for imams, volunteers, and families: building the community's capacity and willingness to receive returning individuals, address stigma directly, and provide ongoing support that extends well beyond the release date

Deradicalization and violence prevention

  • Community-anchored violent extremism and deradicalization programming — culturally grounded, curriculum-based courses designed to be recognized by the Parole Board of Canada as credible program completion. No equivalent Muslim-led program currently exists at the national level
  • Culturally anchored violence prevention programming that addresses root drivers rather than symptoms — building self-identity, social connection, and positive community belonging as protective factors

Convert supports and intra-community diversity

  • Dedicated programming for converts — a frequently overlooked population whose experience of Islam, community belonging, and reintegration differs substantially from those raised Muslim. Supports emphasize normative Islam, navigating diversity within the Muslim community, and building durable religious identity as a stabilizing force

Vetted referral network

  • A curated, actively maintained referral network of vetted partners in employment, housing, and mental health — not a directory, but a set of standing relationships with organizations that have been assessed, trained, and committed to serving this population. Partners are equipped, not just listed
  • Existing practitioners in this space often lack the consistent, credible training required for effective reintegration work. IslamicFamily's training infrastructure raises the floor across the ecosystem — building field-wide capacity, not just organizational capacity
Strand 02

Research and Evaluation

An embedded academic partnership that builds the evidence base CSC currently cannot produce internally — and produces findings that are nationally actionable.

  • Formal co-investigator partnership with Dr. Fahid Qurashi (University of Salford) and Dr. Amin Asfari (University of Regina) — embedded from day one, not appended at the end
  • Disaggregating recidivism outcomes by religion and ethnicity — addressing the measurement gap that currently makes the problem both difficult to quantify and difficult to solve
  • Documenting community-side stigma as a named, measurable barrier — an area absent from existing literature and critical to understanding reintegration outcomes
  • Year 3 research dissemination; Year 5 national symposium and peer-reviewed publication
Strand 03

Policy and Framework Development

Building the institutional infrastructure — standards, credentials, training, and frameworks — that makes this model replicable and that positions CSC as a leader in culturally responsive corrections.

  • Trusted, curriculum-based training for CSC staff — not awareness sessions, but structured, credible professional development in cultural and religious competency that equips front-line staff to reduce institutional friction, improve service uptake, and interact with Muslim individuals with informed confidence
  • Independent third-party validation and community-based advocacy: IslamicFamily provides a credible, trusted voice that can support incarcerated individuals through parole hearings, institutional decision-making, and the transition process — an independent perspective that strengthens, rather than duplicates, CSC's own processes
  • National standards and referral protocols co-developed with CSC — creating the handoff infrastructure that currently does not exist, and giving other community organizations a replicable framework they can adopt without starting from scratch
  • NHQ engagement for national rollout and permanent program framework development
What Works

The principles behind the model

IslamicFamily's approach is not derived from theory alone. It is built from years of direct engagement — inside institutions and in the communities people return to. Five principles emerge consistently from that experience and from the broader evidence base.

01
Community-based advocacy and parole integration
Independent, credible representation that supports incarcerated individuals through their journey — parole hearings, institutional processes, release planning — builds trust, reduces adversarial dynamics, and produces better outcomes for everyone involved.
02
Looking beyond the sentence
Successful reintegration begins inside the institution — preparing not just the individual, but the community that will receive them. Programs that only manage the person in custody and release them into an unprepared community are not solving the problem. IslamicFamily works both sides of the gate simultaneously.
03
Positive self-identity as a program outcome
Faith, community belonging, and a coherent sense of identity are among the strongest protective factors against reoffending. Programming that builds on these strengths — rather than treating them as risks — produces more durable outcomes. This is what culturally grounded programming makes possible.
04
Genuine investment and real relationships
Authentic civil society integration — not transactional referrals — is what moves the needle. People returning from incarceration can distinguish between organizations that are genuinely present and those that are not. IslamicFamily's model is built on relationships that predate this proposal and will outlast it.
05
Field capacity
The people currently doing this work — chaplains, volunteers, imams, community workers — often lack consistent, credible training. IslamicFamily's training infrastructure is designed to raise the floor across the entire field: building shared standards, shared language, and shared competency that benefits every organization working in this space.
Outcomes and Impact

What success looks like — and what the evidence says

Evidence base

The literature on reentry programming is clear: programs that initiate engagement inside the institution and continue into the community produce the strongest outcomes. Meta-analysis across 53 studies found an average 6% recidivism reduction for reentry programs overall — with significantly higher impact for programs that are continuous across custody and community, target high-risk individuals, and last at least 13 weeks. A separate meta-analysis found reductions of up to 62% for programs incorporating pre-release therapeutic services and ongoing post-release support. IslamicFamily's model is designed to the higher end of this evidence base.

Internationally, faith-based correctional programming has demonstrated positive benefit-to-cost ratios consistently — a finding cited in CSC's own January 2026 economic impact review of correctional programs.

The cost case

The annual federal cost of keeping one person incarcerated exceeds $120,000. Preventing even five reoffences in Year 3 recoups the entire Year 3 program cost. By Year 5, a conservative 10% recidivism reduction among 650 cumulative participants represents tens of millions in avoided incarceration costs — many multiples of the total program investment.

This is not speculative arithmetic. It is the same cost-benefit logic that underpins every evidence-based correctional intervention in the literature — and that CSC's own January 2026 economic review of correctional programming confirms.
