Overview

Building Trust in Government Through Collaborative Youth Fellowship Placements

The Civic Youth Fellowship is a paid, placement-based leadership program that connects young people from equity-deserving communities with elected officials and senior city administrators — giving them the tools to understand, navigate, and shape the cities they call home.

Since launching in Edmonton in 2023, the Fellowship has become one of Canada's most recognized models for municipal civic engagement. Now, with the program renewed for five years in Edmonton and expanding to Saskatoon, Big Brothers Big Sisters Calgary and IslamicFamily are proposing to bring the Fellowship to Calgary — adapted for this city's communities, aligned to this Council's priorities, and built to last.

The program runs on a simple premise: cities work better when the people making decisions know the people affected by them. Fellowships are not just internships — they are a structured relationship between a city and its future.

Why It Works for Calgary

For Council, For Youth.

The Fellowship creates measurable value in both directions simultaneously — giving Council and administration a direct pipeline into Calgary's equity-deserving communities, while giving young Calgarians a genuine, paid path into civic life.

For City Council & Administration
  • Direct, structured connection to youth and equity-deserving communities across the city
  • Capacity support in councillor offices: research, communications, constituent engagement, event coordination
  • Capstone projects that deliver genuine value — policy briefs, ward-specific reports, community engagement summaries
  • Builds institutional trust and democratic legitimacy with communities that have historically felt excluded from City Hall
  • Pipeline for future civic talent — many fellows go on to roles in government, nonprofit, and public policy
  • Demonstrates Council's stated commitment to equity, diversity, and trusted government in action
  • Alignment with anti-racism and reconciliation commitments at no additional administrative burden
For Fellows (Youth Ages 19–30)
  • Paid placement at competitive wages — meaningful income, not volunteer experience
  • Four months of pre-placement training covering creating helpful briefing notes, community engagement, policy literacy, and professional skills
  • One-on-one mentorship from civic and community leaders throughout the program
  • Direct working relationship with an elected official or senior administrator
  • Capstone project ownership — tangible work that lives in their portfolio
  • Access to professional networks across government, nonprofit, and business sectors
  • Culturally grounded program — administered by organizations who reflect the communities fellows come from
  • Connection to a national cohort of civic fellows across Edmonton, Saskatoon, and Calgary
Strategic Alignment

Built for this Council's 2027–2030 priorities.

Calgary's Council Priorities for 2027–2030 establish six interconnected focus areas. The Civic Youth Fellowship directly advances four of them — not as a side benefit, but as a core mechanism of delivery.

Council Priority How the Fellowship Delivers
Community Livability & Well-being Fellows work directly on ward-level issues, neighbourhood programming, and community connection projects — strengthening the social fabric of the city from the inside. The program builds a genuine sense of belonging for youth who have often felt outside of civic life.
Trusted & Collaborative Government This is the Fellowship's most direct alignment. Calgary Council's stated outcomes include listening to Calgarians, engaging clearly and simply, and building enhanced partnerships. The Fellowship is a structural mechanism for all three — not a promise, but a practice. It also advances the community indicator around intergovernmental funding secured through CRRF's national partnership.
Safe City Research consistently links civic exclusion, lack of economic opportunity, and disconnection from institutions with increased vulnerability to harm. A Fellowship that creates economic pathways and civic belonging for youth from equity-deserving communities is a prevention strategy — upstream, sustained, and measurable.
Balanced Growth & Evolving Neighbourhoods Fellows bring direct community perspective to ward offices navigating growth and change. They help councillors hear from communities that don't typically show up at public hearings — making neighbourhood evolution more genuinely responsive to residents' needs.
Program Design

How the Fellowship works.

The Fellowship runs on a six-phase annual cycle, from fall recruitment through spring training, summer placement, and a July showcase — before fellows graduate into a sustained alumni network. The full cycle runs October through July.

01
Oct – Dec

Recruitment

Applications open to Calgarians ages 19–30 from equity-deserving communities. Partners lead outreach through community networks, post-secondary institutions, and social media. Interviews and selection completed before year-end.

02
Jan – Apr

Training

Monthly full-day workshops covering creating helpful briefing notes, community engagement, policy literacy, municipal governance, professional skills, and Indigenous values and perspectives. Delivered by community partners and civic professionals.

03
Apr – May

Matching & Orientation

Fellows are matched to a councillor office or senior administration based on skills, interests, and ward priorities. An onboarding session connects fellows with their placement hosts.

04
May – Jul · 10 Weeks

Placement

Full-time, paid 10-week placement at City Hall. Fellows support casework, research, communications, event planning, and constituent engagement — while working on a capstone project.

05
July

Showcase

Fellows present their capstone projects to Council, partners, and the public at City Hall — demonstrating direct contributions to their ward and to the city.

06
Ongoing

Alumni Network

Graduates join a national alumni community, with continued mentorship, networking, and pathways into civic and community careers across Calgary, Edmonton, and Saskatoon.

Program Details

EligibilityAges 19–30, legal right to work in Canada, Calgary residents preferred. Post-secondary education an asset but not required.
CompensationPaid placement at a competitive wage (modelled on Edmonton's $25.25/hr at 36.9 hrs/week). Employment contract with BBBS Calgary.
Placement SitesCouncillor offices, Office of the Mayor, and City of Calgary Executive Leadership Team offices.
Target CommunityYouth from equity-deserving communities, including newcomer, racialized, Indigenous, and underrepresented populations.

Investment

A shared investment in shared leadership.

The Civic Youth Fellowship is designed as a genuine cost-sharing partnership. The City of Calgary's contribution covers 50% of program costs — specifically fellow salaries and one program administration role. The remaining 50% is matched by the Canadian Race Relations Foundation, Big Brothers Big Sisters Calgary, and IslamicFamily. This model has been successfully tested in Edmonton, where it secured a five-year commitment from all parties.

The following cost structure reflects a first-year pilot cohort. Figures below are illustrative pending formal negotiation; the Edmonton model provides the basis for costing.

Partner Contribution Nature of Support Share
City of Calgary 50% Fellow salaries + program administration role (cash) Lead Municipal Partner
Canadian Race Relations Foundation ~17% Cash contribution (national program infrastructure) National Partner
Big Brothers Big Sisters Calgary ~17% Cash + program delivery, recruitment, mentorship infrastructure Local Lead Partner
IslamicFamily ~17% Cash + gift-in-kind (program administration, employer of record) Local Partner
100% Fully Funded Each cohort: estimated $[TBD based on # of fellows] — to be confirmed in partnership agreement
Cost structure note: In Edmonton, the five-year partnership secured $250,000 from CRRF and $250,000 from IslamicFamily, with the City of Edmonton matching at $250,000 — totalling $750,000 over five years for 13+ fellows per cohort. Calgary costs will be calibrated to the number of placement offices confirmed by Council, with a recommended minimum of 8 fellows for a viable first cohort.

About the Edmonton Model

The Civic Youth Fellowship launched in Edmonton in 2023 as a partnership between IslamicFamily, the City of Edmonton, the Africa Centre, ASSIST Community Services Centre, kihêw waciston (MacEwan University), and CRRF. In December 2024, the program was renewed for five years (2025–2030) with a $750,000 commitment across all partners. In 2024, CRRF also launched a parallel program in Saskatoon, confirming the model's transferability to other Canadian cities. Calgary is the natural next step.

Partnership & Recruitment

Who delivers this — and how we find Calgary's fellows.

The Fellowship model works because it is genuinely delivered by community — not parachuted in by institutions. In Edmonton, the program's strength comes from the diversity of its partner organizations, each contributing something the others cannot. The Calgary model replicates this logic, grounded in organizations that are already trusted by the communities the program is designed to serve.

Host Agency · Calgary

Big Brothers Big Sisters Calgary

BBBS Calgary serves as the primary local host agency — holding the employment contract for fellows, leading recruitment and matching, and providing the mentorship infrastructure the program depends on. With an established presence across Calgary's diverse communities and a proven track record in youth development, BBBS Calgary brings the community trust and operational capacity to make the Fellowship work.

Recruitment Strategy

How we find Calgary's first cohort.

Recruitment is the program's most critical function. The Edmonton experience demonstrates that the Fellows selected in year one set the tone for the entire program — and that top candidates come from trusted community networks, not job boards alone. The Calgary recruitment model draws directly on the established Edmonton playbook.

Community Networks

  • BBBS Calgary's existing youth network and mentorship alumni
  • IslamicFamily's newcomer and Muslim community connections across Alberta
  • CACC and Calgary's African-descent community organizations
  • CRRF's national visibility and fellowship alumni network
  • Calgary settlement sector (CCIS, AMSSA member agencies)

Post-Secondary & Institutional

  • University of Calgary — equity and diversity offices, student unions
  • Mount Royal University — community service-learning programs
  • SAIT — newcomer and trades student populations
  • Bow Valley College — largest newcomer student population in the city
  • Ambrose University — faith-based student networks
  • City of Calgary social media and councillor office networks

Candidate Profile

Fellows are not selected on academic credentials — they're selected for community commitment, communication capacity, and curiosity about how their city works. The Edmonton model explicitly welcomes non-traditional applicants: the 2025 cohort included students from industrial design, neuroscience, social work, and business administration. There is no single "right" background for this fellowship. What we look for is people who want to make their city better, and who come from communities that have not had a seat at the table.
