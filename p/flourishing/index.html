Built for Purpose: How IslamicFamily's Social Enterprise Works

The structure behind Flourishing Systems and how it advances IslamicFamily's people-first service work.

Flourishing Systems is a wholly owned subsidiary of IslamicFamily, created to advance IslamicFamily's charitable mission through social enterprise. IslamicFamily built software internally to improve client-centred care and service delivery. That software is now being commercialized — extending the reach of people-first, data justice principles across the sector, and returning revenue to IslamicFamily's charitable work.

What does Flourishing Systems do?

The problem. Intake, case notes, and reporting tools used across the social services sector are built for funders, not for the people seeking help. Clients are forced to retell painful stories at every door. Frontline workers burn out doing administrative work instead of providing care. Data flows upward into reports that justify grants — not into systems that improve lives.

The solution. IslamicFamily spent years building "Transform," a client-centred case management tool for intake, scheduling, case notes, referrals, and reporting — designed around trauma-informed practice and data justice principles. Transform is in active use today by multiple agencies across the sector, with measurable results: organizations using it report significant reductions in time spent on reporting and intake, alongside improved frontline worker satisfaction. Flourishing Systems now develops, supports, and commercializes Transform — and related tools — for other social service organizations.

This work is grounded in IslamicFamily and Flourishing Systems' People-First Data Charter, a public commitment to data justice and OCAP principles (the First Nations standard of ownership, control, access, and possession over data). Read the charters at datacharter.ca.

Does this use IslamicFamily donor funds?

No. The development of Flourishing Systems was funded through restricted innovation grants from funders including the McConnell Foundation, MetaLab, the City of Edmonton, and others — all of whom explicitly supported commercialization as a path to long-term sustainability for the sector. Flourishing Systems receives restricted grant funding only; no general charitable funds flow to the subsidiary.

Flourishing Systems' ongoing revenue comes from selling software to other organizations. Any profits flow back to IslamicFamily. Donor funds support charitable programs. Commercial activity supports the tools that make those programs better.

Why a separate entity?

A for-profit subsidiary protects IslamicFamily's charitable status. Selling software directly from a charity carries legal and compliance risk under CRA, and limits access to organizations that are nonprofits but not registered charities. A separate structure ensures CRA compliance, enables broader access to the tools, and keeps IslamicFamily's charitable focus protected.

This structure was reviewed by independent legal counsel and recommended by Grant Thornton, Pürppl, the Social Enterprise Fund, and others as the optimal approach.

This is a proven model

Charities using commercial activity to fund and extend their mission is an established, well-precedented practice in Canada and internationally — not a novel or risky structure.

Owned for-profit subsidiaries (the structure IslamicFamily uses)

  • Knead Technologies (Calgary) — spun out of the Leftovers Foundation, a food-rescue charity, to commercialize the software it built internally for food rescue logistics. Knead is now a separately incorporated for-profit company that has raised venture investment, while Leftovers Foundation remains a customer and beneficiary of the technology it originated.
  • Oxfam Trading — Oxfam, one of the world's largest charitable federations, owns for-profit trading subsidiaries that generate commercial revenue through retail and merchandising. Profits flow back to fund Oxfam's charitable programs.

Social enterprises operating within a charity

  • Habitat for Humanity ReStores — Habitat affiliates across Canada operate retail stores selling donated building materials and furniture, with proceeds funding affordable home construction.
  • Boyle Street Ventures / Hiregood (Edmonton) — Boyle Street Community Services runs a social enterprise offering moving, cleaning, and property maintenance services, employing people facing barriers to employment, with revenue reinvested into the community.
  • BottleWorks (Ottawa) — a social enterprise of Operation Come Home, employing at-risk youth in bottle collection and recycling, with profits funding the charity's broader youth programs.

Across both models, commercial activity is used deliberately to extend and sustain charitable missions, with profits flowing back to the charitable purpose rather than to individuals. IslamicFamily's structure — a separately incorporated, audited, debt-free subsidiary — sits at the more rigorous end of this spectrum.

How is it governed and safeguarded?

IslamicFamily's board appoints all board members of Flourishing Systems and determines compensation for shared leadership, including the Executive Director. The following safeguards are in place:

  • Single paycheck. The ED, controller, and other shared staff are paid once, by IslamicFamily. Flourishing Systems reimburses IslamicFamily for the use of their time — there is no double-billing.
  • No additional compensation. No leadership receives additional benefits or income through the subsidiary.
  • Arm's-length pricing. IslamicFamily sets fees for shared resources (management time, controller services, office space) based on industry standards, reviewed independently by auditors.
  • No debt permitted. Flourishing Systems is prohibited from taking on debt, limiting financial risk to IslamicFamily.
  • Dual audit. Both entities are independently audited each year.

What's the impact?

Flourishing Systems advances data justice, puts people at the center of their own data, and makes IslamicFamily's social service work more effective and transparent. As the subsidiary matures, it creates a second revenue stream for IslamicFamily — reducing dependency on grants and strengthening long-term sustainability. Donor funds support charitable work. Commercial activity makes that work better.

  • 40% decrease in time organizations spend on reporting for funders.
  • 20% decrease in time required to complete a client intake.
  • 30% of staff time freed up, reducing burnout and redirecting hours from administration to direct client care.
  • 80% frontline worker satisfaction score, with workers reporting tools that finally align with trauma-informed practice.
  • Seamless client engagement — frontline workers can now communicate with clients across multiple languages, platforms, and mediums (text, voice, WhatsApp, and more), meeting people where they are.
  • Richer, real-time data for funders — funders gain meaningful, real-time impact data instead of retroactive, funder-focused reporting that arrives months after the fact.

This work is grounded in IslamicFamily and Flourishing Systems' public People-First Data Charter (datacharter.ca), which names the underlying problem plainly: data collected from people in crisis rarely benefits them, flowing upward into reports that justify grants rather than into systems that improve lives. The Charter commits both organizations to a different standard — data that follows the person, not the program, and that meaningfully drives outcomes and insights rather than retroactive justification.

Common Questions

Answers to questions funders and donors frequently raise.

Money & Conflicts
If Flourishing Systems becomes profitable, who benefits?
Flourishing Systems is a purpose-driven social enterprise. Its primary objective is advancing people-first service, acting sustainably, and returning profit to IslamicFamily's charitable mission. All intercompany agreements are reviewed by the board and auditors for compliance and competitiveness. Profits flow to IslamicFamily's charitable mission — not to individuals.
Who sets the fees IslamicFamily charges Flourishing Systems?
IslamicFamily sets fees based on industry standards. All intercompany agreements are independently reviewed by auditors to ensure they are fair, arm's-length, and compliant.
Could IslamicFamily be liable for Flourishing Systems' debts?
No. Flourishing Systems is prohibited from taking on debt, structurally limiting any financial risk to IslamicFamily.
Mission & IP
Does running a commercial subsidiary distract from IslamicFamily's core work?
No — Flourishing Systems' work directly supports and advances IslamicFamily's charitable programs. Better data tools mean better service delivery. The commercial activity is mission-aligned by design, not a parallel track.
What happens to the software and IP if Flourishing Systems is wound down?
All intellectual property is owned by IslamicFamily. Flourishing Systems operates under a license. If the subsidiary were ever wound down, the IP remains with IslamicFamily.
Could Flourishing Systems be sold to a third party?
IslamicFamily holds majority ownership and therefore determines the disposition of the subsidiary and where any proceeds go. Any such decision would be a board-level decision, subject to the same governance standards.
Client Data & Protection
Does Flourishing Systems have access to IslamicFamily's client data?
Client data is governed by strict standards that exceed sector and government regulations. Data sovereignty and client-centered data principles are foundational to why this software was built in the first place. No client data is transferred to third-party organizations through the software.
Grants & Funding Integrity
Did the original innovation grant funders approve commercialization as an outcome?
Yes. Funders including the McConnell Foundation, MetaLab, and the City of Edmonton explicitly supported commercialization as an intended outcome — seeing it as a path to long-term sustainability for people-first social service technology.
Is IslamicFamily still receiving grants for work that Flourishing Systems then commercializes?
Yes, and this is fully and transparently disclosed. Flourishing Systems receives restricted grant funding only — no general charitable funds. All grant activity is reported in IslamicFamily's audited financial statements and reviewed by auditors each year.
