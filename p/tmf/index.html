Defining the Standard of Care for Muslims in Canadian Corrections

Codifying who is served and what care is owed, training the people who deliver it, and securing its adoption
ToJunaid Qadri, The Muslim Fund
FromOmar Yaqub, IslamicFamily
DateJune 2026
The Ask

A two-year, $300,000 investment ($150,000 per year) for IslamicFamily to define and publish a national standard of care for Muslims in federal corrections, train the community and institutional providers who deliver that care, and secure its adoption through sustained government relations.

The Gap

The questions no one has answered

Across Canada's federal corrections system, the most basic questions about Muslim care have no consistent answer. Who counts as Muslim when a person is admitted? How is a conversion recorded, recognized, or even noticed? When a parent dies, who is permitted a funeral visit, and on what terms? Is halal food available? Is the Quran accessible in a language the person reads? How often does a chaplain actually come?

Today, the answer to every one of these depends on the institution, the staff on shift, and whether a relationship happens to exist. In some facilities Quran access is restricted to English audio only. Halal diet was unavailable in BC institutions as of 2026. Chaplain visits can be as infrequent as once a month. Access to funeral rites and prayer accommodation varies facility by facility.

This is not, at root, a delivery problem. It is a definition problem. Care cannot be delivered consistently, measured honestly, or defended as a right when there is no agreed standard for what the care is, or for who is owed it.

Muslims are roughly 5% of Canada's population and over 9% of the federal prison population. Yet there is no national standard for what Muslim religious care in custody must include. What exists in one institution is absent in the next.

Conversion: the invisible population

Conversions inside custody are frequent and largely untracked. A person who embraces Islam in prison may have no record of it, no chaplain who knows, and no claim to accommodation that follows them through their sentence or their release. The Convert Muslim Foundation's Inside Out report (2020) identifies converts as among the most isolated and underserved within the Muslim prison population. Without a standard for how conversion is recognized and recorded, an entire and growing group is invisible to the system meant to serve it.

Funeral rites and end of life

Among the most acute failures is inconsistent access to funeral visits (Janazah) and to end-of-life and burial accommodation. These are not comfort items. In Islam they are obligations, and being denied them at the moment of a death in the family compounds harm in ways that are difficult to repair, both for the incarcerated person and for the community that receives them afterward.

Why This Comes First

The foundation the rest of the work depends on

IslamicFamily is pursuing a larger, five-year reentry pilot with Correctional Service Canada (CSC) to reduce recidivism among Muslim Canadians through community-delivered reintegration. That pilot, and every reentry effort like it, rests on a foundation that does not yet exist: a clear, agreed standard for who is being served and what care they are owed.

You cannot track recidivism by religion if you cannot reliably identify who is Muslim. You cannot train providers to a standard that has not been written. You cannot hold an institution accountable for accommodation it has never agreed to define. The standards work sits upstream of all of it.

Philanthropy builds the standard. Government scales the delivery. This is the de-risking layer that makes the larger pilot fundable, measurable, and durable.

Government will fund delivery once a credible standard exists. It rarely funds the development of that standard, particularly when the work requires religious and community authority that government does not hold and cannot manufacture. This is precisely the gap philanthropy is positioned to close, and precisely where a focused, relatively modest investment unlocks a far larger public commitment downstream.

The Standard

What gets defined

The project produces a published Muslim Standard of Care for Canadian Corrections: a clear, defensible, citable framework developed with religious, academic, and institutional input, covering the questions that are today left to chance. The Work

Three workstreams over two years

The investment funds three linked workstreams, mapped directly to the budget: academic staffing to build the standard, coordination to turn it into training, and government relations to make it stick.

Workstream 01 — Academic staffing

Standards development

An embedded academic partnership researches, drafts, and validates the standard of care, grounded in normative Islamic scholarship, comparative practice in the UK and US, and the reality of Canadian institutions.

  • Co-investigators Dr. Amin Asfari (University of Regina) and Dr. Fahid Qurashi (University of Salford), working with community scholars on the definitional and conversion-recognition questions
  • A current-state baseline of accommodation across institutions, replacing assumption with evidence
  • Output: a published, citable Muslim Standard of Care framework that government, chaplaincy, and community can all point to
Workstream 02 — Coordination

Training and curriculum

The standard becomes a training curriculum for the two groups who decide whether care actually happens. This is not awareness sessions; it is structured, credible professional development.

  • Community providers (chaplains, imams, volunteers): equipped to deliver to one consistent standard rather than improvising site by site
  • CSC frontline and correctional staff: equipped to recognize and accommodate Muslim religious needs with informed confidence, reducing friction and rights violations
  • Coordination convenes religious, academic, and institutional stakeholders, manages curriculum development, and runs delivery in both regions
Workstream 03 — Government relations

Adoption, data, and accommodation policy

A sustained effort to move the standard from document to practice, pursuing three outcomes as a single package.

  • National adoption. CSC recognition and uptake of the standard across regions, not as an optional reference but as expected practice
  • Data-sharing. Reliable identification and conversion tracking, the precondition for measuring any outcome by religion, including recidivism
  • Accommodation policy. Formal, written policy covering funerals, diet, and prayer, so that basic rights no longer depend on the institution or the individual on shift

Why The Muslim Fund

Securing Muslim interests in the public space

The Muslim Fund exists to create sustainable funding that secures Muslim interests in the public space. Few investments fit that mission more precisely than this one. Prisons are the public institution where Muslims are most over-represented, least visible, and least able to advocate for themselves. Defining and securing their standard of care is securing Muslim interests at the sharpest possible edge.

There is a faith dimension that is hard to overstate. Ensuring that a person can pray, eat what their faith permits, hold a Quran, and bury their dead with dignity is among the most basic obligations a community owes its most vulnerable members. Building a standard that endures, one that outlives any single staff member, government, or grant, is sadaqah jariyah in its truest institutional form: an ongoing benefit that continues to serve long after the initial gift is made.

Government will fund the delivery. It will not fund the development of its own standard, and it cannot supply the religious authority the work requires. That is the gap this investment closes.

The leverage

This $300,000 builds the standard, the curriculum, and the adoption pathway that a multi-million-dollar federal reentry program will rest on. The annual federal cost of keeping one person incarcerated exceeds $120,000. The standard makes it possible to measure, and therefore to reduce, reoffending among a population the system currently cannot even count.

Philanthropy seeds the foundation; government funds the building. This is the highest-leverage point in the entire chain.
